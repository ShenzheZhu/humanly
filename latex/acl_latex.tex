\begin{abstract}
A fluent submission can come from sustained typing, AI polishing, machine translation, or generated text pasted into an editor. We present \textsc{Humanly}, a deployed writing platform that records drafting provenance. Writers or task owners configure AI access, paste rules, length limits, and time windows. Humanly logs typing, paste, focus, selection, and in-platform AI events, then packages the final text, event log, replay, PDF/JSON exports, and verification link into a certificate. Humanly supports personal writing, assigned tasks, share links, and peer review. We describe the architecture and compare Humanly with detector and replay systems. A 240-sample stress test shows that Claude Opus 4.8 fails to recover policy compliance from final text alone. The live system is available at \url{https://app.writehumanly.net/}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Writing authenticity has become a process question. In academic assignments, peer review, and personal certification, the same fluent submission can reflect allowed and disallowed paths. Examples include sustained drafting, grammar-only AI polish, machine translation, chat-based explanation, and generated text moved into an editor. The relevant question is not only whether the final text resembles AI output. It is whether the writer followed the policy governing the task.

Current tools only partially answer that question. Final-text AI detectors estimate whether a submission resembles model output \citep{mitchell2023detectgpt, hans2024binoculars, verma2024ghostbuster}. They work best when a policy bans generation and the submission is fully generated. Mixed workflows break that assumption. Human prose can be wrongly flagged, especially when writing style differs from detector training assumptions \citep{liang2023gpt}; generated prose can also be rewritten, translated, humanized, or prompted to sound human. A detector score alone cannot tell whether the writer followed the task policy.

Process and replay tools provide more context, but they usually start from a host document or assignment platform. They can reconstruct edits or label source categories, yet the task policy, AI-assistant actions, replay, metrics, and verification link often remain separate. For instructors, program chairs, and readers, this produces evidence fragments rather than one policy-bound process record.

\textsc{Humanly} treats authenticity as provenance by linking task policy, write-time events, and reviewable evidence in one record. A writer or task owner configures the environment, the editor records human and AI actions, and the certificate presents the record for review. The system is designed around four requirements. First, the writing policy must be explicit before drafting begins. Second, capture must cover both human edits and in-platform AI actions. Third, the evidence must be inspectable through certificates, logs, and replay. Fourth, the same editor must support personal writing, assigned tasks, public links, guest submissions, and peer review.

AI assistance is a policy choice, not a binary attribute. Humanly has four modes: \textsc{off}, \textsc{polish}, \textsc{chat}, and \textsc{full}. \textsc{Polish} exposes constrained selection rewrites such as grammar fixing and simplification. \textsc{Chat} exposes agent chat without selection-level rewrite actions. \textsc{Full} exposes both. The editor and certificate both show the active policy.

We contribute:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item a deployed writing workspace that records task policy, human actions, and AI actions;
  \item a configuration model for personal, assigned, public-link, and guest writing with explicit AI, paste, time, length, and guest policies;
  \item a certificate pipeline with typed/pasted statistics, AI-use records, replayable history, and public verification;
  \item a 240-sample final-text detector stress test and human-study protocol for evaluating provenance evidence.
\end{enumerate}

The backend stores snapshots, event streams, task enrollments, submissions, AI sessions, AI action logs, files, and certificates. \section{Workflows and Use Cases}
\label{sec:workflows}

Humanly supports three workflows. Each pairs a writing policy with a recorded process and shareable evidence.

\subsection{Personal Writing Certification}

In the personal workflow, a writer creates a document, chooses a writing environment, drafts in Humanly, and generates a certificate. The record reports typed and pasted text, editing time, and in-platform AI use rather than a detector score.

\subsection{Classroom Writing Assignments}

In the classroom workflow, an instructor creates an assigned task, sets AI, paste, time, length, and guest policies, and distributes an invite code or public link. Students write in the policy-bound editor. On submission, the instructor reviews the final text, activity timeline, AI logs, metrics, and certificate.

\subsection{Peer Review}

Peer review is policy-sensitive: reviewers may use AI for grammar or document understanding while remaining responsible for the substantive judgment. Humanly routes review writing through an assigned task with attached paper context, configured AI policy, and a tracked editor. The certificate and replay show whether a review was typed, pasted, AI-assisted, revised, or generated through in-platform tools. The record shows process, not review quality.

\section{Comparison with Existing Systems}
\label{sec:comparison}

We compare Humanly with two classes of systems: final-text detectors and process/replay tools. The comparison follows the four design requirements above: writing environment, policy governance, action capture, and reviewable evidence. Section~\ref{sec:evaluation} then tests whether final text alone can recover policy compliance.

\subsection{Final-Text Detectors}

Final-text detectors such as GPTZero, Pangram, Originality.AI, and Copyleaks score the submitted text after writing ends \citep{gptzero2026detector,pangram2026detector,originality2026detector,copyleaks2026detector}. Such scores help when a task bans generated text and the output is mostly machine-generated. They do not observe the writing process, so they cannot separate allowed AI assistance from policy-violating AI use.

Final text collapses different histories. A human draft polished by AI, a human draft translated by AI, and an AI answer paraphrased by a humanizer can all yield fluent prose. Human prose can also be formal, template-like, or non-native in ways that resemble model output \citep{liang2023gpt}. The policy question is whether the process matched the allowed assistance, not whether the text looks AI-generated.

\subsection{Process and Replay Systems}

Process and replay tools expose document history. Turnitin Clarity provides writing reports and AI-assistance visibility in assignment workflows \citep{turnitin2026clarity}. Grammarly Authorship labels typed, pasted, AI-generated, AI-rephrased, and Grammarly-edited text across supported writing surfaces \citep{grammarly2026authorship}. GPTZero Writing Reports combine detector-adjacent authorship reports with Google Docs process evidence \citep{gptzero2026writingreport}. Google Docs replay tools such as Draftback, Brisk Inspect Writing, Integrito, WritingTrace, and PaperTrail Inspect reconstruct document history from host-editor data \citep{draftback2026replay,brisk2025inspectwriting,integrito2026activityreport,writingtrace2026,papertrail2026inspect}.

Humanly combines configurable writing environments, native AI-use logs, assigned-task enrollment, replay, and certificate analytics in one controlled workspace. We mark a feature as partial when public documentation shows support only in a narrower context, such as a Google Docs extension or an assignment-specific report.

The comparison shows where existing systems stop. Turnitin Clarity is the most direct education baseline because it combines assignment workflows with writing playback and AI-assistance visibility. \subsection{Synthesis}

Final-text detectors answer what the text resembles. Process and replay tools answer what happened inside a host document. Humanly asks whether the recorded process followed the task policy. Because AI assistance is not binary, a course or venue may allow grammar correction, prohibit generation, permit chat-based explanation, or allow full collaboration. The record must include both policy and process.

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

We evaluate Humanly through a final-text stress test, a certificate-based human study protocol, and boundary cases where process records can be bypassed or misread. In all three, the certificate is evidence for human judgment rather than an automatic verdict.

\subsection{Final-Text Detector Stress Test}
\label{sec:detector-eval}

The stress test uses eight policy cases, not one aggregate score. The benchmark policy allows human writing, AI polish, and AI translation, but disallows substantive AI generation of final content. Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-detector-metrics} defines the cases, document-class labels, and policy labels. We adapt prompt-control rules from public research-writing prompt templates such as \citet{leey2026awesomewriting}: transformations must preserve meaning, avoid added facts, and return detector-ready final text. Human-origin cases start from human-written seeds for each length bucket before polish, translation, or AI-style human rewriting.

The reported run contains 240 samples across eight cases and three lengths: social-media-style posts, student assignment responses, and long-form review-style texts. Claude Opus 4.8 \citep{anthropic2026opus48} sees only the final text and predicts one of three document classes: \textsc{human\_only}, \textsc{mixed}, or \textsc{ai\_only}. We score exact document-class accuracy (Layer 1) and policy accuracy after mapping \textsc{human\_only} to \textit{human-compliant} and the other two labels to \textit{AI-suspicious} (Layer 2).

Final text supports similarity judgments, not policy judgments.

\subsection{Human Study}
\label{sec:study}

The human study has writer and reader tasks. The writer task measures usability and policy clarity. The reader task measures whether certificates help participants recover known process labels. Tasks cover peer review, classroom writing, online survey responses, and social-media-style posts. Peer review is the primary setting because it combines writing quality, document understanding, confidentiality, and explicit AI-use policy.

\paragraph{Writer-Side Task.}
Participants write short documents in Humanly under \textsc{off}, \textsc{polish}, \textsc{chat}, and \textsc{full} policies. The study records usability ratings, perceived friction, policy clarity, and trajectory statistics. Surveys measure ease of use, willingness to use Humanly outside the study, and comfort with process recording.

\paragraph{Reader-Side Task.}
Readers inspect writing evidence under three information conditions: final text only, final text plus detector score, and final text plus Humanly certificate/replay. The study measures accuracy against known process labels, confidence, time-to-judgment, perceived fairness, and reported evidence use.

\paragraph{Analysis.}
The analysis reports writer usability, policy clarity, reader accuracy, confidence, and which certificate or replay fields readers use when making judgments.

\subsection{Boundary and Red-Team Analysis}
\label{sec:redteam}

The detector stress test targets final text. Boundary analysis targets the recorder itself. Boundary cases include phone-assisted generation, memorized AI text, manual retyping of an external AI answer, and GUI-agent imitation of human writing. These cases attack the recording workflow rather than the submitted text, so they are separate from the detector benchmark.

These cases refine the trace taxonomy used in the certificate and replay views. The taxonomy separates human-only writing, mostly human writing with some paste, human typing plus AI editing, and AI brainstorming followed by human writing. It also covers AI-generated passages and fully AI-generated text.

\section{Limitations and Ethical Considerations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Process Evidence Is Not Proof.}
Humanly issues process evidence, not an authorship verdict. Certificates support interpretation; they do not decide authorship.

\paragraph{Adversarial Behavior.}
Humanly can block or log some actions, such as paste under task policy, but it cannot prevent every external-AI workflow. A determined user may transcribe external AI output, use voice input, mimic revisions, or route text through another device. Humanly provides process evidence; it does not close every channel for covert assistance.

\paragraph{Privacy and Surveillance.}
Process recording is sensitive. It captures typing behavior, paste activity, focus changes, and AI interactions. Deployments should be transparent to writers, limited to contexts where process evidence is appropriate, and paired with clear access rules for logs, certificates, and replays.

\paragraph{Study Ethics.}
The studies use non-sensitive prompts, disclose process recording, and avoid collecting personally identifying information beyond what is needed for participant payment. If any task involves mild deception, the protocol must justify the necessity and include debriefing. One example is withholding details about paste behavior until the study debrief.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\textsc{Humanly} treats writing authenticity as provenance: did the process comply with the task policy? The platform implements this view through a tracked editor, configurable AI access, assigned-task policy, and verifiable certificates. The stress test shows why final text is insufficient: Claude Opus 4.8 misses many policy-violating cases and flags many compliant ones. Humanly does not replace human judgment; it supplies process evidence for that judgment.

\subsection{Data and Preprocessing}

The benchmark contains 240 final-text samples: eight cases, three length buckets, and ten matched task sets per bucket. The buckets correspond to social-media-style posts, student assignment responses, and long-form review-style texts. Each row stores task metadata, source language, policy label, expected document class, text paths, construction notes, license notes, and word count.

Source collection and generation are separated. All inputs are frozen as local files before generation; source URLs remain only as provenance metadata. Human English seeds supply C1 and C2 inputs. Human non-English seeds supply C3 inputs. C4 uses newly collected human-written AI-style texts. N1 is generated from the matched task topic. N2 rewrites the matched N1 draft. N3 generates non-English AI text and translates it into English. N4 lightly edits the matched N1 draft through human editing. We remove markup, boilerplate, and templates from seed material but do not polish seed prose. Detector inputs include only rows marked \texttt{ready}.

\subsection{Metric Definitions}

The evaluation has two metric layers.

\noindent\textbf{Layer 1: Final-text document class.} A prediction is correct only if it exactly matches the expected document class: \textsc{human\_only}, \textsc{mixed}, or \textsc{ai\_only}. This layer asks whether a detector can recover the kind of text it is looking at, without judging whether the workflow was allowed.

\noindent\textbf{Layer 2: Policy compliance.} We map detector outputs into binary policy evidence: \textsc{human\_only} becomes \textit{human-compliant}; \textsc{mixed} and \textsc{ai\_only} become \textit{AI-suspicious}. We compare that mapped prediction against the benchmark policy label. True positives are non-compliant samples predicted as AI-suspicious. False negatives are non-compliant samples predicted as human-compliant. True negatives are compliant samples predicted as human-compliant. False positives are compliant samples predicted as AI-suspicious.
