\begin{abstract}
A fluent submission can result from sustained typing, AI polishing, machine translation, or generated text pasted into an editor. We present \textsc{Humanly}, a deployed platform that records writing provenance during drafting. Writers or task owners configure AI access, paste rules, length limits, and time windows. Humanly logs typing, paste, focus, selection, and in-platform AI events, then packages the final text, event record, replay, PDF/JSON exports, and verification link into a certificate. The platform supports personal writing, assigned tasks, public share links, and peer review. We describe the architecture, compare Humanly with text detectors and replay systems, and report a 240-sample stress test showing that Claude Opus 4.8 cannot recover policy compliance from final text alone. The live system is available at \url{https://app.writehumanly.net/}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

In many writing settings, authenticity depends on the drafting process, not the final text alone. A fluent draft may come from sustained human typing, limited AI polishing, machine translation, or off-platform generation. Instructors, program chairs, and readers therefore need more than a label for whether text ``is AI.'' They need to know which policy governed the task and what process produced the submission.

Final-text AI detectors estimate whether a text resembles model output \citep{mitchell2023detectgpt, hans2024binoculars, verma2024ghostbuster}. They work best when a policy bans generation and a submission is fully generated. They fail to resolve mixed workflows. Human prose can be wrongly flagged, especially when a writer's style differs from detector training assumptions \citep{liang2023gpt}; generated prose can also be rewritten, translated, humanized, or prompted to sound human. A detector score alone cannot tell whether the writer followed the task policy.

\textsc{Humanly} treats authenticity as provenance by linking task policy, write-time events, and reviewable evidence. A writer or task owner configures the environment, the editor records human and AI actions, and the certificate presents the record for review.

AI assistance is a policy choice. Humanly has four modes: \textsc{off}, \textsc{polish}, \textsc{chat}, and \textsc{full}. \textsc{Polish} exposes constrained selection rewrites such as grammar fixing and simplification. \textsc{Chat} exposes agent chat without selection-level rewrite actions. \textsc{Full} exposes both. The policy appears in the editor and in the evidence trail.

We contribute:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item a deployed platform for policy-bound human-AI writing;
  \item a configuration model for personal, assigned, public-link, and guest writing with explicit AI, paste, time, and length policies;
  \item a certificate pipeline with typed/pasted statistics, AI-use records, replayable history, and public verification;
  \item a 240-sample final-text detector stress test and human-study protocol for evaluating provenance evidence.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Evidence store.}
The backend stores snapshots, event streams, task enrollments, submissions, AI sessions, AI action logs, files, and certificates. PostgreSQL/TimescaleDB stores high-frequency events. Redis handles caching and background jobs. WebSocket channels carry live activity. Derived views turn raw events into logs, timelines, analytics, and certificates.

Before certificate generation or task submission, Humanly flushes pending editor events and saves the latest snapshot (Figure~\ref{fig:certificate-surface}). \section{Workflows and Use Cases}
\label{sec:workflows}

Humanly supports three workflows, each pairing a writing policy with a recorded process and shareable evidence.

\subsection{Personal Writing Certification}

In the personal workflow, a writer creates a document, chooses a writing environment, drafts in Humanly, and generates a certificate. The record reports typed and pasted text, editing time, and in-platform AI use rather than a detector score.

\subsection{Classroom Writing Assignments}

In the classroom workflow, an instructor creates an assigned task, sets the AI mode, paste rule, time window, writing timer, length limit, and guest-submission policy, then distributes an invite code or public link. Students write in the policy-bound editor. On submission, the instructor reviews the final text, activity timeline, AI logs, metrics, and certificate.

\subsection{Peer Review}

Peer review is policy-sensitive: reviewers may use AI for grammar or document understanding while remaining responsible for the substantive judgment. Humanly routes review writing through an assigned task with attached paper context, configured AI policy, and a tracked editor. The certificate and replay show whether a review was typed, pasted, AI-assisted, revised, or generated through in-platform tools. The record does not grade review quality; it shows how the review was produced.

\section{Comparison with Existing Systems}
\label{sec:comparison}

Humanly differs from both final-text detectors and process/replay systems. Detectors judge text; replay tools reconstruct edits; Humanly binds task policy to recorded actions. Section~\ref{sec:evaluation} tests whether final text alone recovers policy compliance.

\subsection{Final-Text Detectors}

Final-text detectors such as GPTZero, Pangram, Originality.AI, and Copyleaks score the submitted text after writing ends \citep{gptzero2026detector,pangram2026detector,originality2026detector,copyleaks2026detector}. These scores are informative when a task bans generated text and the output is mostly machine-generated. They do not observe the writing process, so they cannot separate allowed AI assistance from policy-violating AI use.

This limitation is structural. A human draft polished by AI, a human draft translated by AI, and an AI answer paraphrased by a humanizer can all yield fluent prose. Human prose can also be formal, template-like, or non-native in ways that resemble model output \citep{liang2023gpt}. The policy question is whether the process matched the allowed assistance, not whether the text looks AI-generated.

\subsection{Process and Replay Systems}

Process and replay tools expose document history. Turnitin Clarity provides writing reports and AI-assistance visibility in assignment workflows \citep{turnitin2026clarity}. Grammarly Authorship labels typed, pasted, AI-generated, AI-rephrased, and Grammarly-edited text across supported writing surfaces \citep{grammarly2026authorship}. GPTZero Writing Reports combine detector-adjacent authorship reports with Google Docs process evidence \citep{gptzero2026writingreport}. Google Docs replay tools such as Draftback, Brisk Inspect Writing, Integrito, WritingTrace, and PaperTrail Inspect reconstruct document history from host-editor data \citep{draftback2026replay,brisk2025inspectwriting,integrito2026activityreport,writingtrace2026,papertrail2026inspect}.

Humanly combines configurable writing environments, native AI-use logs, assigned-task enrollment, replay, and certificate analytics in one controlled workspace. \subsection{Synthesis}

Final-text detectors estimate what the text resembles. Process and replay tools show part of what happened inside a document. Humanly records whether the process complied with the task policy. Because AI assistance is not binary, a course or venue may allow grammar correction, prohibit generation, permit chat-based explanation, or allow full collaboration. The record must therefore include both policy and process.

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

Evaluation separates text-only prediction, human use, and adversarial boundaries. The final-text stress test asks whether text-only predictors recover task-policy compliance from submitted text alone. The human-study protocol asks whether writers and readers can use process evidence in assigned-writing and peer-review tasks. Boundary analysis identifies cases where process evidence can be bypassed or misread. In all three, the certificate is a record for human judgment rather than an automatic verdict.

\subsection{Final-Text Detector Stress Test}
\label{sec:detector-eval}

The stress test uses case-based evaluation rather than aggregate accuracy alone. The benchmark policy allows human writing, AI polish, and AI translation, but disallows substantive AI generation of final content. Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-detector-metrics} defines the eight cases, document-class labels, and policy labels. We adapt prompt-control rules from public research-writing prompt templates such as \citet{leey2026awesomewriting}. Transformations must preserve meaning, avoid added facts, and return detector-ready final text. Human-origin cases start from human-written seeds for each length bucket before polish, translation, or AI-style human rewriting.

The reported run contains 240 samples across eight cases and three lengths: social-media-style posts, student assignment responses, and long-form review-style texts. Claude Opus 4.8 \citep{anthropic2026opus48} sees only the final text and predicts one of three document classes: \textsc{human\_only}, \textsc{mixed}, or \textsc{ai\_only}. We score exact document-class accuracy (Layer 1) and policy accuracy after mapping \textsc{human\_only} to \textit{human-compliant} and the other two labels to \textit{AI-suspicious} (Layer 2).

Final text supports similarity judgments, not policy judgments.

\subsection{Human Study}
\label{sec:study}

The human study measures writer usability, policy clarity, and reader judgment with certificate evidence. Tasks cover peer review, classroom writing, online survey responses, and social-media-style posts. Peer review is the primary setting because it combines writing quality, document understanding, confidentiality, and explicit AI-use policy.

\paragraph{Writer-Side Task.}
Participants write short documents in Humanly under selected policies: \textsc{off}, \textsc{polish}, \textsc{chat}, and \textsc{full}. The study records usability ratings, perceived friction, policy clarity, and trajectory statistics. Surveys measure ease of use, willingness to use Humanly in real settings, and comfort with process recording.

\paragraph{Reader-Side Task.}
Readers inspect writing evidence under three information conditions: final text only, final text plus detector score, and final text plus Humanly certificate/replay. The study measures accuracy against known process labels, confidence, time-to-judgment, perceived fairness, and reported evidence use.

\paragraph{Analysis.}
The analysis reports writer usability, policy clarity, reader accuracy, confidence, and which certificate or replay fields readers use when making judgments.

\subsection{Boundary and Red-Team Analysis}
\label{sec:redteam}

The detector stress test isolates final-text failures under policy cases. Boundary analysis studies where process evidence itself can be bypassed or misread. Boundary cases include phone-assisted generation, memorized AI text, manual retyping of an external AI answer, and GUI-agent imitation of human writing. These cases target the recording workflow rather than the submitted text and are therefore separate from the detector benchmark.

These cases refine the trace taxonomy used in the certificate and replay views. The taxonomy separates human-only writing, mostly human writing with some paste, human typing plus AI editing, and AI brainstorming followed by human writing. It also covers AI-generated portions and fully AI-generated text.

\section{Limitations and Ethical Considerations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Process evidence is not proof.}
Humanly issues process evidence, not an authorship verdict. Certificates support interpretation; they do not decide authorship.

\paragraph{Adversarial behavior.}
Humanly can block or log some actions, such as paste under task policy, but it cannot prevent every external-AI workflow. A determined user may transcribe external AI output, use voice input, mimic revisions, or route text through another device. Humanly is evidence infrastructure, not a complete anti-cheating mechanism.

\paragraph{Privacy and surveillance.}
Process recording is sensitive. It captures typing behavior, paste activity, focus changes, and AI interactions. Deployments should be transparent to writers, limited to contexts where process evidence is appropriate, and paired with clear access rules for logs, certificates, and replays.

\paragraph{Study ethics.}
The studies use non-sensitive prompts, disclose process recording, and avoid collecting personally identifying information beyond what is needed for participant payment. If any task involves mild deception, the protocol must justify the necessity and include debriefing. One example is withholding details about paste behavior until the study debrief.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\textsc{Humanly} treats writing authenticity as provenance: did the process comply with the task policy? The platform implements this view through a tracked editor, configurable AI access, assigned-task policy, and verifiable certificates. The stress test shows why final text is insufficient: Claude Opus 4.8 misses many policy-violating cases and flags many compliant ones. Humanly does not replace human judgment; it gives that judgment a process record.

\subsection{Data and Preprocessing}

The benchmark contains 240 final-text samples: eight cases, three length buckets, and ten matched task sets per bucket. The buckets correspond to social-media-style posts, student assignment responses, and long-form review-style texts. Each row stores task metadata, source language, policy label, expected document class, text paths, construction notes, license notes, and word count.

Source collection and generation are separated. Before generation, all inputs are frozen as local files under the detector stress-test data directory; source URLs remain only as provenance metadata. Human English seeds provide C1 and the source texts for C2 polishing. Human non-English seeds provide C3 translation inputs. C4 contains newly collected human-written AI-style texts. N1 is generated from the matched task topic. N2 rewrites the matched N1 draft, N3 generates non-English AI text and translates it into English, and N4 lightly edits the matched N1 draft through human editing. Markup, boilerplate, and templates are removed from seed material before transformation, but seed prose is not stylistically polished. Detector inputs include only rows marked \texttt{ready}.

\subsection{Metric Definitions}

The evaluation has two metric layers.

\noindent\textbf{Layer 1: Final-text document class.} A prediction is correct only if it exactly matches the expected document class: \textsc{human\_only}, \textsc{mixed}, or \textsc{ai\_only}. This layer asks whether a detector can recover the kind of text it is looking at, without judging whether the workflow was allowed.

\noindent\textbf{Layer 2: Policy compliance.} We map detector outputs into binary policy evidence: \textsc{human\_only} becomes \textit{human-compliant}; \textsc{mixed} and \textsc{ai\_only} become \textit{AI-suspicious}. We then compare that mapped prediction against the benchmark policy label. True positives are non-compliant samples predicted as AI-suspicious. False negatives are non-compliant samples predicted as human-compliant. True negatives are compliant samples predicted as human-compliant. False positives are compliant samples predicted as AI-suspicious.
