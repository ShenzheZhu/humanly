\begin{abstract}
Final text alone cannot distinguish human drafting, AI polishing, AI translation, and imported generation. \textsc{Humanly} is a deployed writing platform for policy-bound process evidence. Task owners configure AI access, paste rules, time/length limits, and guest access. During drafting, the editor records typing, paste, focus, selection, and in-platform AI actions; certificates expose metrics, logs, replay, and public verification. The platform supports personal writing, assigned tasks, public links, guest submissions, and peer review. In a 240-sample stress test, GPTZero and Pangram detect most disallowed generation but mark 25--27\% of compliant cases as suspicious, showing why process evidence should accompany final-text scoring. A live deployment is available at \url{https://app.writehumanly.net/}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

In assignments, peer review, and certification, authenticity turns on production history: who wrote what, which tools were used, and what policy governed that use. A clean final document can come from human drafting, in-platform AI polishing, outside generation, or some mixture of these paths. Reviewers therefore need evidence about the writing process, not only a score assigned to the submitted text.

Post-hoc detectors score whether submitted text resembles model output \citep{mitchell2023detectgpt, hans2024binoculars, verma2024ghostbuster}. They help when generation is banned and violations are machine-written. Mixed workflows break that assumption: human prose may be flagged when style differs from detector training data \citep{liang2023gpt}, while generated prose can be rewritten, translated, or humanized. A detector score is not a policy record.

Document-replay tools add edit history, but they rarely make the policy record explicit. A timeline may show that text appeared or changed. It may not show the assignment policy, allowed AI mode, in-platform AI requests, certificate metrics, or public verification link. Readers must reconstruct whether the observed history satisfies the task rules.

\textsc{Humanly} records policy-bound provenance directly. Policy is set before writing; human and in-platform AI actions are recorded during drafting; certificates expose logs, replay, metrics, and verification after drafting. The same record spans personal, assigned, public-link, guest, and peer-review workflows.

\textsc{Humanly} has been designed, developed, and deployed around two goals (Figure~\ref{fig:workflow}):
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{High flexibility and compatibility.} Humanly supports multiple writing environments rather than a single fixed policy. Personal writers and task owners can configure resources, AI access, paste rules, time and length limits, guest access, and submission mode. Across those settings, Humanly supports personal writing, assigned tasks, public links, guest submissions, and peer review, with AI access ranging from no AI to polish-only, chat-only, and full assistance.
  \item \textbf{High accountability and transparency.} Humanly is a native human-AI writing platform rather than an after-the-fact detector. Its editor records fine-grained human actions and in-platform AI actions during drafting, including typing, paste, focus, selection, AI chat, rewrite suggestions, and accept/reject decisions. Certificates, logs, replay, metrics, and public verification expose this process record for later inspection.
\end{enumerate}
The paper describes the deployed system and reports a 240-sample stress test showing where final-text predictors fail under policy labels.

\section{Workflows and Use Cases}
\label{sec:workflows}

Across workflows, Humanly separates three roles that are often collapsed in writing tools. A policy owner sets the rules, a writer drafts under those rules, and a reader later reviews the evidence. The same editor, event stream, certificate, and replay serve all three roles, but the interface changes according to whether the user is configuring, writing, submitting, or auditing.

\subsection{Peer Review}

Peer review is policy-sensitive: AI may be allowed for grammar or document understanding, while reviewers retain responsibility for judgment. In Humanly, program chairs assign a paper, an AI policy, and any constraints before reviewers begin. Reviewers then work in the tracked workspace with the paper and allowed AI tools available in the same surface. Certificates and replay show whether a review was typed, pasted, AI-assisted, revised, or generated through in-platform tools. The record describes production, not quality; it helps chairs inspect whether a review followed policy before deciding how to weigh it.

\subsection{Classroom Writing Assignments}

Instructors configure tasks, distribute invite codes or public links, and set whether guest submissions are allowed. Students see the AI, paste, time, and length rules before writing, then draft in the same tracked editor used for personal tasks. Submissions include timelines, AI logs, metrics, and certificates. This lets instructors inspect compliance with the stated policy rather than infer intent from final prose or run a detector after submission.

\subsection{Personal Writing Certification}

For self-certification, writers set policy, draft in Humanly, and export PDF/JSON evidence. This workflow is useful when a writer wants to attach process evidence to a grant statement, public post, application essay, or other document outside an assigned task. The certificate reports typed text, pasted text, editing time, and in-platform AI use rather than a detector score.

\section{Comparison with Existing Systems}
\label{sec:comparison}

Existing systems differ by where they observe writing. Final-text detectors observe only the submitted document. Replay tools observe revision history inside another editor, usually Google Docs. Humanly observes a policy-bound production process, where the rules, writing actions, AI interactions, and certificate are generated by the same system.

\subsection{Final-Text Detectors}

Final-text detectors such as GPTZero, Pangram, Originality.AI, and Copyleaks score submitted text after writing ends \citep{gptzero2026detector,pangram2026detector,originality2026detector,copyleaks2026detector}. They can flag likely machine-written submissions, but without write-time evidence they conflate allowed AI assistance with policy violations.

Final text collapses production histories. A human draft polished by AI, a human draft translated by AI, and an AI answer paraphrased by a humanizer may all look fluent. Human prose may also be formal, template-like, or non-native in ways that resemble model output \citep{liang2023gpt}. The policy question is process, not style.

\subsection{Process and Replay Systems}

Process and replay tools reconstruct document history rather than scoring text alone. Turnitin Clarity combines assignments, writing reports, and AI-assistance visibility \citep{turnitin2026clarity}. Grammarly Authorship labels typed, pasted, AI-generated, AI-rephrased, and Grammarly-edited text across supported writing surfaces \citep{grammarly2026authorship}. GPTZero Writing Reports combine AI review, detection, and Google Docs process evidence \citep{gptzero2026writingreport,gptzero2026turnitinbrisk}. Other replay systems, including Draftback, Brisk Inspect Writing, Integrito, WritingTrace, and PaperTrail Inspect, derive timelines from Google Docs data \citep{draftback2026replay,brisk2025inspectwriting,integrito2026activityreport,writingtrace2026,papertrail2026inspect}.

Table~\ref{tab:feature-comparison} compares systems by environment, governance, capture, and evidence. Final-text detectors provide no write-time environment. Host-document replay tools inherit the surrounding editor and usually lack task policy or native AI logs. Humanly places policy, writing, AI assistance, capture, replay, and certification in one workspace. A partial mark denotes narrower support, such as a browser extension, Google Docs report, or assignment-specific report.

The distinction is not only whether a system shows writing history. In Humanly, the same event stream gates policy, logs AI actions, computes certificate metrics, and drives replay. This coupling gives each event policy context. A reader can interpret a paste event, an AI rewrite, or a long pause relative to the rules active during drafting, rather than treating each artifact as a separate clue.

\subsection{Synthesis}

Final-text detectors classify style after writing ends, and replay tools reconstruct history from a host document. Humanly instead treats policy as part of the writing environment. The system does not decide whether a writer is honest; it exposes whether recorded actions match the policy that governed the task.

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

Evaluation asks three questions aligned with the system claim. First, can final-text-only predictors infer policy compliance across mixed human-AI writing paths? Second, can readers use certificates and replay to interpret the writing process without treating them as automatic verdicts? Third, which adversarial behaviors fall outside the observed workspace and should be disclosed as limits?

\subsection{Final-Text Detector Stress Test}
\label{sec:detector-eval}

The detector stress test uses eight policy cases rather than a binary human-vs-AI label. Human writing, AI polish, and AI translation are compliant; substantive AI generation is not. Predictors infer policy compliance from final text alone. Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-detector-metrics} defines the cases and labels. Prompted transformations preserve meaning and avoid added facts, following public research-writing prompt templates such as \citet{leey2026awesomewriting}. Human-origin cases start from human-written seeds before polish, translation, or AI-style human rewriting.

The stress test has 240 samples: eight cases, three length buckets, and ten matched task sets. Buckets correspond to social-media posts, student assignment responses, and long review-style texts. Predictors receive final text only and return \textsc{human\_only}, \textsc{mixed}, or \textsc{ai\_only}. We evaluate Claude Opus 4.8 \citep{anthropic2026opus48}, GPTZero \citep{gptzero2026detector}, and Pangram \citep{pangram2026detector}. Metrics are exact document-class accuracy (Layer 1) and policy accuracy after mapping \textsc{human\_only} to \textit{human-compliant} and the other labels to \textit{AI-suspicious} (Layer 2).

Both also mark all 30 C4 human-written AI-style samples suspicious. Final-text predictors can triage likely AI-origin text, but they do not answer how AI entered the writing process.

\subsection{Human Study}
\label{sec:study}

The study evaluates two roles that matter for deployment. Writers use Humanly under configured AI policies and report whether the workflow is understandable, usable, and acceptable. Readers inspect the resulting evidence packages and decide what process they believe produced the document. Peer review is the primary scenario because it combines document understanding, accountable judgment, confidentiality, and an explicit AI policy.

\paragraph{Writer-Side Task.}
Writers complete short Humanly tasks under \textsc{off}, \textsc{polish}, \textsc{chat}, and \textsc{full} policies. Each condition changes the available editor controls, so the task tests whether writers can understand and follow the policy while still completing the writing task. Writer-side measures are usability, friction, policy clarity, comfort with recording, reuse intent, and event-derived writing statistics.

\paragraph{Reader-Side Task.}
Readers inspect three information conditions: final text only, final text plus detector score, and final text plus Humanly certificate/replay. The comparison tests whether process evidence changes judgments beyond what readers infer from prose style or detector output. Reader-side measures are process-label accuracy, confidence, time-to-judgment, perceived fairness, and cited evidence fields.

\paragraph{Analysis.}
Analysis compares writing experience across AI modes and reader judgment across evidence conditions. We will report whether policy-constrained writing remains usable, whether evidence packages improve process interpretation, and which certificate or replay fields readers rely on. Open-ended responses identify fields that readers use, ignore, or misread.

\subsection{Boundary and Red-Team Analysis}
\label{sec:redteam}

Final-text stress tests do not cover every threat model. External-device generation, memorized AI output, manual retyping, and GUI-agent imitation bypass or imitate the workspace rather than changing detector accuracy. These cases are not failures of final-text classification alone; they test what the event stream can observe, what it cannot observe, and which claims a certificate should avoid.

These boundaries organize trace categories for certificates and replay: human-only drafting, human drafting with paste, AI editing, AI brainstorming, AI-generated passages, and fully AI-generated text. They also motivate future behavior-based detectors that operate on pauses, burst lengths, paste events, selection edits, and in-platform AI logs rather than final text alone.

\section{Limitations and Ethical Considerations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Process Evidence Is Not Proof.}
A certificate records observed in-platform actions; it is not an authorship verdict. It can show that a writer typed, pasted, revised, or used in-platform AI under a known policy, but it cannot prove private intention or exclude every outside channel. Policy judgment remains with reviewers, instructors, chairs, or other readers who decide how to interpret the record.

\paragraph{Adversarial Behavior.}
Humanly records in-platform actions and enforces configured controls, such as paste blocking, but cannot close every external channel. A user may transcribe external AI output, use voice input, mimic revisions, or route text through another device. Certificates support review of observed activity, not proof that no outside assistance occurred.

\paragraph{Privacy and Surveillance.}
Because process recording captures typing behavior, paste activity, focus changes, and AI interactions, deployments require writer-facing disclosure, purpose limits, and clear access rules for logs, certificates, and replays. Writers should know which events are collected, who can inspect them, how long they are retained, and whether public verification exposes only a certificate summary or a richer replay.

\paragraph{Study Ethics.}
Study prompts avoid sensitive topics, disclose process recording, and limit personally identifying information to participant payment. Any withheld detail about paste behavior requires justification and debriefing.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\textsc{Humanly} shifts writing-authenticity review from final-text scoring to policy-bound process evidence. It records writer and in-platform AI actions under configured task policies, then packages those records as certificates, logs, and replay. The stress test illustrates why this shift matters: GPTZero and Pangram catch most non-compliant generation but flag many compliant samples, while Opus 4.8 misses many policy violations. Humanly does not automate judgment. It supplies the process record reviewers need to ask whether the writing path matched the policy.

\subsection{Data and Preprocessing}

The benchmark contains 240 samples: eight cases \(\times\) three length buckets \(\times\) ten matched task sets. Buckets cover social-media-style posts, student responses, and long review-style texts. Each row records task metadata, source language, policy label, document class, text path, construction note, license note, and word count.

Inputs are frozen as local files before generation; source URLs remain metadata only. Human-origin seeds supply C1--C3: English for C1/C2 and non-English for C3. C4 contains collected human-written AI-style texts. Non-compliant samples use matched topics: direct AI drafting (N1), rewriting or humanizing (N2), non-English AI drafting plus translation (N3), and light human editing of N1 drafts (N4). Preprocessing removes markup, boilerplate, and templates without polishing the prose. Detector runs include only \texttt{ready} rows. GPTZero and Pangram each returned 240/240 valid outputs with no missing samples or request errors; GPTZero used API version \texttt{2026-05-11-base}.

\subsection{Metric Definitions}

Two metric layers are reported.

\noindent\textbf{Layer 1: Final-text document class.} A prediction is correct only if it exactly matches the expected document class: \textsc{human\_only}, \textsc{mixed}, or \textsc{ai\_only}. For \(N\) samples with expected class \(d_i\) and predicted class \(\hat{d}_i\),
\[
\mathrm{DocAcc}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\mathbf{1}[\hat{d}_i=d_i].
\]
This layer scores construction history, not policy.

\noindent\textbf{Layer 2: Policy compliance.}
We first map detector outputs to binary policy evidence.
\textsc{human\_only} becomes human-compliant (\(\hat{y}=0\)).
\textsc{mixed} and \textsc{ai\_only} become AI-suspicious (\(\hat{y}=1\)).
The benchmark label is \(y=0\) for compliant samples and \(y=1\) for non-compliant samples.
This gives the following confusion matrix:
\[
\begin{array}{c|cc}
 & \hat{y}=1 & \hat{y}=0 \\
\hline
y=1 & TP & FN \\
y=0 & FP & TN \\
\end{array}
\]

\noindent\textbf{Policy accuracy.} Policy accuracy is the fraction of samples on which the binary policy prediction is correct:
\[
\mathrm{PolicyAcc}=\frac{TP+TN}{TP+FP+TN+FN}.
\]

\noindent\textbf{False-positive rate.} FPR is the fraction of compliant samples incorrectly flagged as AI-suspicious:
\[
\mathrm{FPR}=\frac{FP}{FP+TN}.
\]

\noindent\textbf{False-negative rate.} FNR is the fraction of non-compliant samples incorrectly predicted as human-compliant:
\[
\mathrm{FNR}=\frac{FN}{FN+TP}.
\]
