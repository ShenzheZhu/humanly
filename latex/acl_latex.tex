\begin{abstract}
The same final text can result from sustained drafting, AI polish, translation, or generated text moved into an editor. We present \textsc{Humanly}, a deployed platform that records writing policy and composition actions. Writers or task owners set AI access, paste, length, time, and guest-submission rules. Humanly captures typing, paste, focus, selection, and in-platform AI events, then exports text, metrics, logs, replay, PDF/JSON records, and public verification. Personal writing, assigned tasks, public links, guest submissions, and peer review share this pipeline. In a 240-sample stress test, GPTZero and Pangram catch most disallowed generation but falsely flag at least 25\% of compliant cases. The live system is available at \url{https://app.writehumanly.net/}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Academic assignments, peer review, and authenticity certification often depend on process, not text alone. Similar submissions may reflect sustained drafting, grammar-only AI polish, machine translation, chat-based explanation, or generated text moved into an editor.

Post-hoc detectors answer a narrower question: whether the submitted text resembles model output \citep{mitchell2023detectgpt, hans2024binoculars, verma2024ghostbuster}. That signal helps only when generation is banned and the output is mostly machine-written. Mixed workflows do not fit that premise. Human prose may be wrongly flagged, especially when style differs from detector training assumptions \citep{liang2023gpt}; generated prose can be rewritten, translated, humanized, or prompted to sound human. A detector score alone cannot determine policy compliance.

Replay tools recover document history but usually depend on a host editor or assignment platform. They reconstruct edits or label source categories, while task policy, AI-assistant actions, metrics, replay, and verification remain separate. Readers must reconcile artifacts rather than inspect one policy-bound record.

\textsc{Humanly} links task policy and write-time events in one provenance record. Users set policy before writing; the platform records human and in-platform AI actions during drafting; certificates present logs, replay, metrics, and verification after drafting. The record covers personal, assigned, public-link, guest, and peer-review workflows.

AI assistance is governed by policy, not by a binary label. Humanly implements four modes: \textsc{off}, \textsc{polish}, \textsc{chat}, and \textsc{full}. \textsc{Polish} enables constrained selection rewrites such as grammar fixing and simplification. \textsc{Chat} enables agent chat without selection-level rewrite actions. \textsc{Full} enables both. The editor and certificate show the active policy.

We contribute:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item a tracked writing workspace binding task policy to human and AI actions;
  \item a configuration model spanning personal, assigned, public-link, guest, and peer-review writing;
  \item a certificate format linking typed/pasted statistics, AI-use records, replay, and verification;
  \item a 240-sample stress test of final-text predictors under policy labels.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Evidence Store.}
The backend persists snapshots, event streams, enrollments, submissions, AI sessions, AI action logs, files, and certificates. PostgreSQL/TimescaleDB stores high-frequency events; Redis handles caching and background jobs; WebSocket channels carry live activity. Humanly derives logs, timelines, typed/pasted statistics, AI-use statistics, and replay data from these records.

\paragraph{Certificate and Verification.}
Before certificate generation or task submission, Humanly flushes pending editor events and saves the latest snapshot (Figure~\ref{fig:certificate-surface}). The certificate binds the final text to event counts, typed/pasted and editing metrics, AI-use metrics, a verification token, and a signed payload. Writers can export PDF or JSON; readers can inspect the certificate and permitted replay/logs.

\section{Workflows and Use Cases}
\label{sec:workflows}

All workflows share the same sequence: set policy, write, and review evidence. They differ in who sets the policy and who reads the record.

\subsection{Peer Review}

Peer review is policy-sensitive: reviewers may use AI for grammar or document understanding while remaining responsible for the substantive judgment. Humanly treats review writing as an assigned task with paper context, AI policy, and a tracked editor. Certificates and replay show whether a review was typed, pasted, AI-assisted, revised, or generated through in-platform tools. The record describes production, not review quality.

\subsection{Classroom Writing Assignments}

An instructor creates an assigned task, sets AI, paste, time, length, and guest policies, and distributes an invite code or public link. Students write in the policy-bound editor. On submission, the instructor reviews the text, timeline, AI logs, metrics, and certificate.

\subsection{Personal Writing Certification}

A personal writer creates a document, selects AI and paste policy, drafts in Humanly, and generates a certificate. The certificate reports typed and pasted text, editing time, and in-platform AI use rather than a detector score.

\section{Comparison with Existing Systems}
\label{sec:comparison}

Final-text detectors and process/replay tools differ in what they observe: only the submitted text, host-document history, or a policy-bound writing process.

\subsection{Final-Text Detectors}

Final-text detectors such as GPTZero, Pangram, Originality.AI, and Copyleaks score the submitted text after writing ends \citep{gptzero2026detector,pangram2026detector,originality2026detector,copyleaks2026detector}. Such scores are informative when a task bans generation and the output is mostly machine-written. Because they do not observe writing, they cannot separate allowed AI assistance from policy-violating AI use.

Final text collapses different histories. A human draft polished by AI, a human draft translated by AI, and an AI answer paraphrased by a humanizer may all yield fluent prose. Human prose may also be formal, template-like, or non-native in ways that resemble model output \citep{liang2023gpt}. The policy question is whether the process matched the allowed assistance, not whether the text looks machine-written.

\subsection{Process and Replay Systems}

Process and replay tools reconstruct document history. Turnitin Clarity provides writing reports and AI-assistance visibility in assignment workflows \citep{turnitin2026clarity}. Grammarly Authorship labels typed, pasted, AI-generated, AI-rephrased, and Grammarly-edited text across supported writing surfaces \citep{grammarly2026authorship}. GPTZero Writing Reports combine AI review, detection, and Google Docs process evidence \citep{gptzero2026writingreport,gptzero2026turnitinbrisk}. Google Docs replay tools such as Draftback, Brisk Inspect Writing, Integrito, WritingTrace, and PaperTrail Inspect derive timelines from host-editor data \citep{draftback2026replay,brisk2025inspectwriting,integrito2026activityreport,writingtrace2026,papertrail2026inspect}.

Humanly combines configurable environments, native AI-use logs, assigned-task enrollment, replay, and certificate analytics in one controlled workspace. Replay alone does not answer the policy question. Turnitin Clarity is the strongest education baseline: it combines assignment workflows with writing playback and AI-assistance visibility. Grammarly Authorship is the strongest source-attribution baseline: it labels typed, pasted, AI-generated, and AI-rephrased text. WritingTrace and PaperTrail Inspect focus on writing history rather than detector probability.

Humanly keeps policy, AI actions, writing events, replay, and certificate metrics in a single write-time record. A writer or task owner sets the policy before writing; the editor enforces it during composition; the certificate is generated from the same event stream.

\subsection{Synthesis}

Final-text detectors answer what the text resembles. Process and replay tools answer what happened inside a host document. Humanly asks whether the recorded process followed the task policy. Because AI assistance is not binary, the record must include both policy and process.

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

Evaluation targets two questions: whether final text recovers policy compliance, and whether readers interpret process evidence without treating it as a verdict. Boundary cases identify where the record can be bypassed or misread.

\subsection{Final-Text Detector Stress Test}
\label{sec:detector-eval}

We use eight policy cases rather than one human-vs-AI label. The benchmark policy allows human writing, AI polish, and AI translation, but disallows substantive AI generation; this design tests whether a final-text-only predictor can substitute for process evidence. Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-detector-metrics} defines the cases, document-class labels, and policy labels. Prompted transformations preserve meaning, avoid added facts, and return detector-ready final text, following public research-writing prompt templates such as \citet{leey2026awesomewriting}. Human-origin cases start from human-written seeds before polish, translation, or AI-style human rewriting.

The stress test contains 240 samples: eight cases, three lengths, and ten matched task sets per length. Lengths correspond to social-media-style posts, student assignment responses, and long-form review-style texts. Each predictor receives only final text; outputs are normalized to \textsc{human\_only}, \textsc{mixed}, or \textsc{ai\_only}. Predictors are Claude Opus 4.8 \citep{anthropic2026opus48}, GPTZero \citep{gptzero2026detector}, and Pangram \citep{pangram2026detector}. We score exact document-class accuracy (Layer 1) and policy accuracy after mapping \textsc{human\_only} to \textit{human-compliant} and the other labels to \textit{AI-suspicious} (Layer 2).

Table~\ref{tab:detector-results} separates document-class and policy errors. Opus 4.8 assigns the correct three-class label to 82/240 samples and misses many disallowed AI-origin cases. GPTZero and Pangram make 205/240 and 208/240 correct policy decisions, missing only 3 and 2 of 120 non-compliant samples. At the document-class layer, however, both remain below 0.50 accuracy and mark 32 and 30 of 120 compliant samples as suspicious. C4 is the hardest compliant case: humans intentionally write in an AI-like style, and both detectors flag all 30 C4 samples. Final text may identify obvious AI-origin documents, but it cannot explain whether a writing process followed the task policy.

\subsection{Human Study}
\label{sec:study}

The human study tests interpretability, not automated judgment. Writers complete tasks under configured AI policies; readers infer process labels from evidence. Tasks cover peer review, classroom writing, online survey responses, and social-media-style posts. Peer review is the primary setting because it combines writing quality, document understanding, confidentiality, and explicit AI policy.

\paragraph{Writer-Side Task.}
Participants write short documents in Humanly under \textsc{off}, \textsc{polish}, \textsc{chat}, and \textsc{full} policies. Writer-side measures cover usability, friction, policy clarity, comfort with recording, reuse intent, and event-derived writing statistics.

\paragraph{Reader-Side Task.}
Readers inspect three information conditions: final text only, final text plus detector score, and final text plus Humanly certificate/replay. Reader-side measures cover process-label accuracy, confidence, time-to-judgment, perceived fairness, and reported evidence use.

\paragraph{Analysis.}
Analysis compares writer measures across AI modes and reader measures across information conditions. Open-ended responses identify helpful or confusing certificate and replay fields.

\subsection{Boundary and Red-Team Analysis}
\label{sec:redteam}

The detector stress test targets final text; boundary analysis targets capture assumptions. Boundary cases include phone-assisted generation, memorized AI text, manual retyping of an external AI answer, and GUI-agent imitation of human writing. These cases attack observability and are separate from the detector benchmark.

These cases also refine the trace taxonomy behind certificates and replay. The taxonomy separates human-only writing, mostly human writing with paste, human typing plus AI editing, AI brainstorming followed by human writing, AI-generated passages, and fully AI-generated text.

\section{Limitations and Ethical Considerations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Process Evidence Is Not Proof.}
Humanly issues process evidence, not an authorship verdict. Certificates support interpretation; they do not decide authorship.

\paragraph{Adversarial Behavior.}
Humanly blocks or logs some policy-governed actions, such as paste, but it cannot prevent every external-AI workflow. A determined user may transcribe external AI output, use voice input, mimic revisions, or route text through another device. The record captures in-platform process; it cannot rule out every covert channel.

\paragraph{Privacy and Surveillance.}
Process recording is sensitive. It captures typing behavior, paste activity, focus changes, and AI interactions. Deployments therefore need writer-facing transparency, context limits, and clear access rules for logs, certificates, and replays.

\paragraph{Study Ethics.}
Study prompts are non-sensitive, process recording is disclosed, and personally identifying information is limited to participant payment. If a task involves mild deception, such as withholding details about paste behavior, the protocol must justify the necessity and include debriefing.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\textsc{Humanly} makes writing authenticity reviewable as policy-bound process evidence. Its tracked editor, configurable AI access, assigned-task flow, and certificates turn writing events into an inspectable record. The stress test shows why final text is insufficient: GPTZero and Pangram catch most disallowed generation but still flag many compliant cases, while Opus 4.8 misses many policy violations. Humanly does not replace judgment; it gives reviewers an auditable record.

\subsection{Data and Preprocessing}

The benchmark contains 240 final-text samples: eight cases, three length buckets, and ten matched task sets per bucket. Length buckets correspond to social-media-style posts, student assignment responses, and long-form review-style texts. Each row stores task metadata, source language, policy label, document class, text paths, construction notes, license notes, and word count.

Source collection and generation are separated. Inputs are frozen as local files before generation; source URLs remain only as metadata. Human-origin seeds supply C1--C3: English for C1 and C2, and non-English for C3. C4 uses newly collected human-written AI-style texts. For non-compliant cases, N1 creates a topic-matched draft; N2 rewrites N1; N3 translates non-English AI text; and N4 lightly human-edits N1. Seed material is stripped of markup, boilerplate, and templates, but not polished. Detector inputs include only \texttt{ready} rows. GPTZero and Pangram each produced 240 valid outputs, with no missing rows or request errors.

\subsection{Metric Definitions}

The evaluation uses two metric layers.

\noindent\textbf{Layer 1: Final-text document class.} A prediction is correct only if it exactly matches the expected document class: \textsc{human\_only}, \textsc{mixed}, or \textsc{ai\_only}. This layer measures whether a detector can recover how the final text was constructed, independent of whether the workflow was allowed.

\noindent\textbf{Layer 2: Policy compliance.} Detector outputs are mapped into binary policy evidence: \textsc{human\_only} becomes \textit{human-compliant}; \textsc{mixed} and \textsc{ai\_only} become \textit{AI-suspicious}. The mapped prediction is compared with the benchmark policy label, with non-compliance as the positive class. Under this convention, TP/FN are non-compliant samples predicted as AI-suspicious/human-compliant, and TN/FP are compliant samples predicted as human-compliant/AI-suspicious.
