\begin{abstract}
Final text alone cannot distinguish among sustained drafting, AI polish, translation, and text generated elsewhere. \textsc{Humanly} is a deployed writing platform that records process evidence under task policy. Before writing, policies set AI access, paste rules, length limits, time limits, and guest access. During writing, the editor records typing, paste, focus, selection, and in-platform AI actions. PDF/JSON certificates bind final text to metrics, logs, replay, and public verification across personal writing, assigned tasks, public links, guest submissions, and peer review. In a 240-sample stress test, GPTZero and Pangram detect most disallowed generation but mark 25--27\% of compliant cases as suspicious. The live system is available at \url{https://app.writehumanly.net/}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Academic assignments, peer review, and authenticity certification depend on how text was produced, not final text alone. Similar submissions can come from sustained drafting, grammar-only AI polish, machine translation, chat-based explanation, or imported generation.

Post-hoc detectors score whether submitted text resembles model output \citep{mitchell2023detectgpt, hans2024binoculars, verma2024ghostbuster}. They are informative when generation is banned and violations are machine-written. Mixed workflows break that assumption: human prose may be flagged when style differs from detector training data \citep{liang2023gpt}, while generated prose can be rewritten, translated, or humanized. A detector score alone cannot determine policy compliance.

Document-replay tools reconstruct host-editor history, but policy, AI-assistant actions, metrics, replay, and verification often remain separate. Readers must reconcile artifacts instead of inspecting one policy-bound record.

\textsc{Humanly} binds task policy to write-time events in one provenance record. Policy is set before writing. Human and in-platform AI actions are recorded during drafting. Certificates present logs, replay, metrics, and verification after drafting. The same record spans personal, assigned, public-link, guest, and peer-review workflows.

The design follows three requirements. Policy is fixed before drafting, AI assistance occurs inside the recorded workspace, and readers receive one evidence package rather than separate logs, scores, and exports.

Four AI policies control the editor: \textsc{off} hides AI controls, \textsc{polish} exposes only selection rewrites, \textsc{chat} exposes only chat, and \textsc{full} exposes both. The editor displays the active policy during writing; the certificate reports it after drafting.

We present:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item a controlled editor that enforces task policy while recording human and AI actions;
  \item task flows for personal writing, assigned work, public links, guest submissions, and peer review;
  \item certificate and replay artifacts that report writing statistics, AI-use records, logs, and verification;
  \item a 240-sample stress test showing where final-text predictors fail under policy labels.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Evidence Store.}
The backend stores snapshots, event streams, enrollments, submissions, files, AI sessions, AI actions, and certificates. PostgreSQL/TimescaleDB handles high-frequency events. Redis backs jobs and caching; WebSocket channels carry live updates. Logs, timelines, typed/pasted statistics, AI-use statistics, and replay data are derived from these records.

\paragraph{Certificate and Verification.}
Before certificate generation or task submission, Humanly flushes pending editor events and saves the latest snapshot (Figure~\ref{fig:certificate-surface}). The certificate binds final text to event counts, typed/pasted and editing metrics, AI-use metrics, a verification token, and a signed payload. Readers inspect a PDF or JSON certificate and any permitted replay/logs.

\section{Workflows and Use Cases}
\label{sec:workflows}

Across workflows, policy is fixed before writing and evidence is reviewed after writing. Workflows differ by policy owner and record reader.

\subsection{Peer Review}

Peer review is policy-sensitive: AI may be allowed for grammar or document understanding, but reviewers remain responsible for judgment. Humanly treats review writing as an assigned task with paper context, AI policy, and a tracked editor. Certificates and replay show whether a review was typed, pasted, AI-assisted, revised, or generated through in-platform tools. The record describes production, not review quality.

\subsection{Classroom Writing Assignments}

Instructors assign writing tasks, distribute invite codes or public links, and review submissions with timelines, AI logs, metrics, and certificates. The workflow gives both sides a shared record of the assigned policy and the submitted process.

\subsection{Personal Writing Certification}

For self-certification, writers set policy, draft in Humanly, and generate a certificate reporting typed and pasted text, editing time, and in-platform AI use, not a detector score.

\section{Comparison with Existing Systems}
\label{sec:comparison}

Existing approaches differ by observation scope: submitted text, host-document history, or a policy-bound writing process.

\subsection{Final-Text Detectors}

Final-text detectors such as GPTZero, Pangram, Originality.AI, and Copyleaks score submitted text after writing ends \citep{gptzero2026detector,pangram2026detector,originality2026detector,copyleaks2026detector}. They are most informative when generation is banned and violations are largely machine-written. Without write-time evidence, they cannot separate allowed AI assistance from policy-violating AI use.

Final text collapses different histories. A human draft polished by AI, a human draft translated by AI, and an AI answer paraphrased by a humanizer may all yield fluent prose. Human prose may also be formal, template-like, or non-native in ways that resemble model output \citep{liang2023gpt}. The policy question is whether the process matched the allowed assistance, not whether the text looks machine-written.

\subsection{Process and Replay Systems}

Process and replay tools move beyond detector scores by reconstructing document history. Turnitin Clarity combines assignments, writing reports, and AI-assistance visibility \citep{turnitin2026clarity}. Grammarly Authorship labels typed, pasted, AI-generated, AI-rephrased, and Grammarly-edited text across supported writing surfaces \citep{grammarly2026authorship}. GPTZero Writing Reports combine AI review, detection, and Google Docs process evidence \citep{gptzero2026writingreport,gptzero2026turnitinbrisk}. Draftback, Brisk Inspect Writing, Integrito, WritingTrace, and PaperTrail Inspect derive timelines from Google Docs data \citep{draftback2026replay,brisk2025inspectwriting,integrito2026activityreport,writingtrace2026,papertrail2026inspect}.

Table~\ref{tab:feature-comparison} compares systems by environment, governance, capture, and evidence. Existing tools cover these dimensions unevenly: they reconstruct edits, label text origins, or support assigned tasks, but rarely combine them. Humanly combines configurable policy, native AI logs, assigned-task enrollment, replay, and certificate analytics in one controlled workspace. A partial mark denotes support only in a narrower context, such as a Google Docs extension or an assignment-specific report.

\subsection{Synthesis}

Final-text detectors answer what the text resembles. Process and replay tools answer what happened inside a host document. Humanly records the missing premise: what policy applied and what actions occurred.

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

The evaluation covers final-text prediction, reader interpretation, and boundary cases.

\subsection{Final-Text Detector Stress Test}
\label{sec:detector-eval}

Our benchmark uses eight policy cases, not a binary human-vs-AI label. Human writing, AI polish, and AI translation are compliant; substantive AI generation is not. The test measures whether final-text predictors recover policy compliance without process evidence. Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-detector-metrics} defines the cases and labels. Prompted transformations preserve meaning and avoid added facts, following public research-writing prompt templates such as \citet{leey2026awesomewriting}. Human-origin cases start from human-written seeds before polish, translation, or AI-style human rewriting.

The stress test has 240 samples: eight cases, three length buckets, and ten matched task sets. Buckets correspond to social-media posts, student assignment responses, and long review-style texts. Each predictor sees only final text and returns \textsc{human\_only}, \textsc{mixed}, or \textsc{ai\_only}. We evaluate Claude Opus 4.8 \citep{anthropic2026opus48}, GPTZero \citep{gptzero2026detector}, and Pangram \citep{pangram2026detector}. Metrics are exact document-class accuracy (Layer 1) and policy accuracy after mapping \textsc{human\_only} to \textit{human-compliant} and the other labels to \textit{AI-suspicious} (Layer 2).

Their main error is false positives: 32 and 30 of 120 compliant samples are marked suspicious. All 30 C4 samples, where humans intentionally write in an AI-like style, fall into this error class. Final-text predictors flag many AI-origin documents, but they do not verify policy compliance.

\subsection{Human Study}
\label{sec:study}

The human study separates production from interpretation. Writers draft under configured AI policies; readers judge selected drafts from evidence packages. Measures cover task completion, policy clarity, and certificate/replay interpretation. Peer review is primary because it combines writing quality, document understanding, confidentiality, and explicit AI policy.

\paragraph{Writer-Side Task.}
Writers complete short Humanly tasks under \textsc{off}, \textsc{polish}, \textsc{chat}, and \textsc{full} policies. Writer measures cover usability, friction, policy clarity, comfort with recording, reuse intent, and event-derived writing statistics.

\paragraph{Reader-Side Task.}
Readers inspect three information conditions: final text only, final text plus detector score, and final text plus Humanly certificate/replay. Reader measures cover process-label accuracy, confidence, time-to-judgment, perceived fairness, and cited evidence fields.

\paragraph{Analysis.}
We compare writer measures across AI modes and reader measures across information conditions. Open-ended responses identify which certificate and replay fields readers use or misread.

The detector stress test targets final text; boundary analysis targets capture assumptions. Cases include phone-assisted generation, memorized AI text, manual retyping of an external AI answer, and GUI-agent imitation of human writing. These cases test observability rather than detector accuracy.

They also motivate the trace taxonomy behind certificates and replay: human-only writing, mostly human writing with paste, AI editing, AI brainstorming, AI-generated passages, and fully AI-generated text.

\section{Limitations and Ethical Considerations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Process Evidence Is Not Proof.}
Certificates support interpretation; they are not authorship verdicts.

\paragraph{Adversarial Behavior.}
Humanly logs or blocks in-platform actions, such as paste when policy disallows it, but it cannot prevent every external-AI workflow. A determined user may transcribe external AI output, use voice input, mimic revisions, or route text through another device. The record captures in-platform activity; it cannot rule out every covert channel.

\paragraph{Privacy and Surveillance.}
Process recording is sensitive because it captures typing behavior, paste activity, focus changes, and AI interactions. Deployments require writer-facing transparency, context limits, and clear access rules for logs, certificates, and replays.

\paragraph{Study Ethics.}
Study prompts avoid sensitive topics, process recording is disclosed, and personally identifying information is limited to participant payment. If the protocol withholds details about paste behavior, it requires justification and debriefing.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\textsc{Humanly} treats writing authenticity as a question of policy and recorded process. A tracked editor, AI policy controls, assigned-task flow, and certificates show how a draft was produced. The stress test shows why final text is insufficient: GPTZero and Pangram detect most non-compliant generation yet flag many compliant cases, while Opus 4.8 misses many policy violations. Humanly does not replace judgment; it gives reviewers the process record behind the text.

\subsection{Data and Preprocessing}

The benchmark contains 240 samples: eight cases \(\times\) three length buckets \(\times\) ten matched task sets. Buckets cover social-media-style posts, student responses, and long review-style texts. Rows store task metadata, source language, policy label, document class, text path, construction note, license note, and word count.

Inputs are frozen as local files before generation; source URLs remain only as metadata. Human-origin seeds supply C1--C3: English for C1/C2 and non-English for C3. C4 uses newly collected human-written AI-style texts. Non-compliant cases use matched topics: N1 creates an AI draft, N2 rewrites it, N3 translates non-English AI text, and N4 lightly human-edits N1. Seed material is stripped of markup, boilerplate, and templates, but not polished. Detector inputs include only \texttt{ready} rows. GPTZero and Pangram each returned 240/240 valid outputs.

\subsection{Metric Definitions}

We report two metric layers.

\noindent\textbf{Layer 1: Final-text document class.} A prediction is correct only if it exactly matches the expected document class: \textsc{human\_only}, \textsc{mixed}, or \textsc{ai\_only}. This layer measures final-text construction independent of policy.

\noindent\textbf{Layer 2: Policy compliance.} We map detector outputs to binary policy evidence: \textsc{human\_only} becomes \textit{human-compliant}; \textsc{mixed} and \textsc{ai\_only} become \textit{AI-suspicious}. Non-compliance is the positive class. TP/FN count non-compliant samples predicted as AI-suspicious/human-compliant; TN/FP count compliant samples predicted as human-compliant/AI-suspicious.
