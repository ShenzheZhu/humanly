\begin{abstract}
Final text alone cannot distinguish sustained drafting from AI polish, translation, or imported generation. \textsc{Humanly} is a deployed writing platform that records process evidence under task policy. Before writing, policies define AI access, paste rules, length and time limits, and guest access. During writing, the editor records typing, paste, focus, selection, and in-platform AI actions. PDF/JSON certificates bind final text to metrics, logs, replay, and public verification across personal, assigned, public-link, guest, and peer-review workflows. In a 240-sample stress test, GPTZero and Pangram detect most disallowed generation but mark 25--27\% of compliant cases as suspicious. The live system is available at \url{https://app.writehumanly.net/}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Academic assignments, peer review, and authenticity certification depend on production history. The relevant distinction is whether a writer drafted, polished, translated, consulted AI, or imported generated text. Final text often cannot answer.

Post-hoc detectors score whether submitted text resembles model output \citep{mitchell2023detectgpt, hans2024binoculars, verma2024ghostbuster}. They help when generation is banned and violations are machine-written. Mixed workflows break that assumption: human prose may be flagged when style differs from detector training data \citep{liang2023gpt}, while generated prose can be rewritten, translated, or humanized. A detector score is not a policy record.

Document-replay tools add history, but policy, AI-assistant actions, metrics, replay, and verification often remain separate. Readers must reconcile artifacts instead of inspecting one policy-bound record.

\textsc{Humanly} makes policy-bound provenance explicit. Policy is set before writing; human and in-platform AI actions are recorded during drafting; certificates expose logs, replay, metrics, and verification after drafting. The same record spans personal, assigned, public-link, guest, and peer-review workflows.

Three constraints guide the design: policy precedes drafting, AI assistance stays inside the recorded workspace, and readers inspect one evidence package rather than separate logs, scores, and exports.

Four AI policies control the editor: \textsc{off} hides AI controls, \textsc{polish} exposes only selection rewrites, \textsc{chat} exposes only chat, and \textsc{full} exposes both. The editor displays the active policy during writing; the certificate reports it after drafting.

We present:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item a controlled editor that enforces task policy while recording human and AI actions;
  \item task flows for personal writing, assigned work, public links, guest submissions, and peer review;
  \item certificate and replay artifacts that connect final text to writing statistics, AI-use records, logs, and verification;
  \item a 240-sample stress test quantifying final-text predictor errors under policy labels.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Evidence Store.}
The backend stores snapshots, event streams, enrollments, submissions, files, AI sessions, AI actions, and certificates. PostgreSQL/TimescaleDB handles high-frequency events; Redis backs jobs and caching; WebSocket channels carry live updates. Stored records drive logs, timelines, typed/pasted statistics, AI-use statistics, and replay data.

\paragraph{Certificate and Verification.}
Before certificate generation or task submission, Humanly flushes pending editor events and saves the latest snapshot (Figure~\ref{fig:certificate-surface}). The certificate binds final text to event counts, typed/pasted and editing metrics, AI-use metrics, a verification token, and a signed payload. Readers inspect the PDF or JSON certificate and any permitted replay/logs.

\section{Workflows and Use Cases}
\label{sec:workflows}

Each workflow fixes policy before writing and reviews evidence afterward. They differ in policy owner and record reader.

\subsection{Peer Review}

Peer review is policy-sensitive: AI may be allowed for grammar or document understanding, while reviewers retain responsibility for judgment. Humanly treats review writing as an assigned task with paper context, AI policy, and a tracked editor. Certificates and replay show whether a review was typed, pasted, AI-assisted, revised, or generated through in-platform tools. The record describes production, not quality.

\subsection{Classroom Writing Assignments}

Instructors configure tasks, distribute invite codes or public links, and review submissions through timelines, AI logs, metrics, and certificates. Both sides see the assigned policy and the submitted process.

\subsection{Personal Writing Certification}

For self-certification, writers set policy, draft in Humanly, and generate a certificate reporting typed text, pasted text, editing time, and in-platform AI use rather than a detector score.

\section{Comparison with Existing Systems}
\label{sec:comparison}

Existing systems differ by where they observe writing: submitted text, host-document history, or a policy-bound production process.

\subsection{Final-Text Detectors}

Final-text detectors such as GPTZero, Pangram, Originality.AI, and Copyleaks score submitted text after writing ends \citep{gptzero2026detector,pangram2026detector,originality2026detector,copyleaks2026detector}. They can flag likely machine-written submissions, but without write-time evidence they conflate allowed AI assistance with policy violations.

Final text collapses production histories. A human draft polished by AI, a human draft translated by AI, and an AI answer paraphrased by a humanizer may all look fluent. Human prose may also be formal, template-like, or non-native in ways that resemble model output \citep{liang2023gpt}. The policy question is process, not style.

\subsection{Process and Replay Systems}

Process and replay tools reconstruct document history rather than scoring text alone. Turnitin Clarity combines assignments, writing reports, and AI-assistance visibility \citep{turnitin2026clarity}. Grammarly Authorship labels typed, pasted, AI-generated, AI-rephrased, and Grammarly-edited text across supported writing surfaces \citep{grammarly2026authorship}. GPTZero Writing Reports combine AI review, detection, and Google Docs process evidence \citep{gptzero2026writingreport,gptzero2026turnitinbrisk}. Other replay systems, including Draftback, Brisk Inspect Writing, Integrito, WritingTrace, and PaperTrail Inspect, derive timelines from Google Docs data \citep{draftback2026replay,brisk2025inspectwriting,integrito2026activityreport,writingtrace2026,papertrail2026inspect}.

Table~\ref{tab:feature-comparison} compares systems by environment, governance, capture, and evidence. The surveyed tools reconstruct edits, label text origins, or support assigned tasks, but rarely combine these functions. Humanly combines configurable policy, native AI logs, assigned-task enrollment, replay, and certificate analytics in one controlled workspace. A partial mark denotes narrower support, such as a Google Docs extension or an assignment-specific report.

\subsection{Synthesis}

Final-text detectors answer what the text resembles. Process and replay tools answer what happened inside a host document. Humanly adds the missing premise: what policy applied and what actions occurred.

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

Evaluation covers final-text prediction, reader interpretation, and boundary cases.

\subsection{Final-Text Detector Stress Test}
\label{sec:detector-eval}

We use eight policy cases rather than a binary human-vs-AI label. Human writing, AI polish, and AI translation are compliant; substantive AI generation is not. The task is to infer policy compliance from final text alone. Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-detector-metrics} defines the cases and labels. Prompted transformations preserve meaning and avoid added facts, following public research-writing prompt templates such as \citet{leey2026awesomewriting}. Human-origin cases start from human-written seeds before polish, translation, or AI-style human rewriting.

The stress test has 240 samples: eight cases, three length buckets, and ten matched task sets. Buckets correspond to social-media posts, student assignment responses, and long review-style texts. Predictors receive final text only and return \textsc{human\_only}, \textsc{mixed}, or \textsc{ai\_only}. We evaluate Claude Opus 4.8 \citep{anthropic2026opus48}, GPTZero \citep{gptzero2026detector}, and Pangram \citep{pangram2026detector}. Metrics are exact document-class accuracy (Layer 1) and policy accuracy after mapping \textsc{human\_only} to \textit{human-compliant} and the other labels to \textit{AI-suspicious} (Layer 2).

GPTZero and Pangram are more policy-accurate, with 205/240 and 208/240 correct decisions, and miss only 3 and 2 of 120 non-compliant samples. Their main error is false positives: 32 and 30 of 120 compliant samples are marked suspicious. C4 exposes this failure: all 30 human-written AI-style samples are marked suspicious. Final-text predictors can flag AI-origin documents, but they do not verify policy compliance.

\subsection{Human Study}
\label{sec:study}

The human study separates production from interpretation. Writers draft under configured AI policies; readers judge selected drafts from evidence packages. Peer review is the primary task because it combines writing quality, document understanding, confidentiality, and AI policy.

\paragraph{Writer-Side Task.}
Writers complete short Humanly tasks under \textsc{off}, \textsc{polish}, \textsc{chat}, and \textsc{full} policies. Writer-side measures are usability, friction, policy clarity, comfort with recording, reuse intent, and event-derived writing statistics.

\paragraph{Reader-Side Task.}
Readers inspect three information conditions: final text only, final text plus detector score, and final text plus Humanly certificate/replay. Reader-side measures are process-label accuracy, confidence, time-to-judgment, perceived fairness, and cited evidence fields.

\paragraph{Analysis.}
Analysis compares writer measures across AI modes and reader measures across information conditions. Open-ended responses identify which certificate and replay fields readers use or misread.

\subsection{Boundary and Red-Team Analysis}
\label{sec:redteam}

The detector stress test targets final text; boundary analysis targets capture assumptions. Cases include phone-assisted generation, memorized AI text, manual retyping of an external AI answer, and GUI-agent imitation of human writing. They test observability, not detector accuracy.

Boundary cases also define a trace taxonomy for certificates and replay: human-only writing, mostly human writing with paste, AI editing, AI brainstorming, AI-generated passages, and fully AI-generated text.

\section{Limitations and Ethical Considerations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Process Evidence Is Not Proof.}
Certificates support interpretation rather than automatic authorship verdicts.

\paragraph{Adversarial Behavior.}
Humanly logs or blocks in-platform actions, such as paste when policy disallows it, but cannot prevent every external-AI workflow. A determined user may transcribe external AI output, use voice input, mimic revisions, or route text through another device. The record captures in-platform activity; covert channels remain possible.

\paragraph{Privacy and Surveillance.}
Process recording is sensitive: it captures typing behavior, paste activity, focus changes, and AI interactions. Deployments require writer-facing transparency, context limits, and clear access rules for logs, certificates, and replays.

\paragraph{Study Ethics.}
Study prompts avoid sensitive topics, process recording is disclosed, and personally identifying information is limited to participant payment. Any withholding about paste behavior requires justification and debriefing.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\textsc{Humanly} treats writing authenticity as policy and process. Its editor, AI controls, task flow, and certificates show how a draft was produced. The stress test shows why final text is insufficient: GPTZero and Pangram detect most non-compliant generation yet flag many compliant cases, while Opus 4.8 misses many policy violations. Humanly does not replace judgment; it gives reviewers the process record behind the text.

\subsection{Data and Preprocessing}

The benchmark contains 240 samples: eight cases \(\times\) three length buckets \(\times\) ten matched task sets. Buckets cover social-media-style posts, student responses, and long review-style texts. Each row records task metadata, source language, policy label, document class, text path, construction note, license note, and word count.

Inputs are frozen as local files before generation; source URLs remain metadata only. Human-origin seeds supply C1--C3: English for C1/C2 and non-English for C3. C4 contains collected human-written AI-style texts. Non-compliant samples use matched topics: direct AI drafting (N1), rewriting or humanizing (N2), non-English AI drafting plus translation (N3), and light human editing of N1 drafts (N4). Seed material is stripped of markup, boilerplate, and templates, but not polished. Detector runs include only \texttt{ready} rows. GPTZero and Pangram each returned 240/240 valid outputs.

\subsection{Metric Definitions}

Two metric layers are reported.

\noindent\textbf{Layer 1: Final-text document class.} A prediction is correct only if it exactly matches the expected document class: \textsc{human\_only}, \textsc{mixed}, or \textsc{ai\_only}. This layer scores construction history, not policy.

\noindent\textbf{Layer 2: Policy compliance.} Detector outputs are mapped to binary policy evidence: \textsc{human\_only} becomes \textit{human-compliant}; \textsc{mixed} and \textsc{ai\_only} become \textit{AI-suspicious}. Non-compliance is the positive class. True positives and false negatives are non-compliant samples predicted as AI-suspicious or human-compliant; true negatives and false positives are compliant samples predicted as human-compliant or AI-suspicious.
