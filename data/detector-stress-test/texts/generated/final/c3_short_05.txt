We all know that tripeiro designates an inhabitant or native of the city of Porto. Some dictionaries, such as Houaiss and Aulete, say it is pejorative; others, such as the dictionary of the Lisbon Academy of Sciences or Priberam, do not. This subject was the object of this question. According to one narrative, Porto residents acquired the nickname tripeiro because they were left only with the tripe, after providing the clean meat to the expedition that went to conquer Ceuta in 1415. In another version, it was in 1384, when a small fleet left Porto loaded with provisions to go to the aid of Lisbon, which had been under siege by the Castilians for some months. But none of the sites that tell these narratives presents any evidence that tripeiro was already used in those remote times; some sites openly say that linking the nickname tripeiro to those royal expeditions is mere legend. So my question is: how far back can the designation tripeiro, applied to Porto residents, be traced? When does it first appear in the written record? And was it they themselves who so nicknamed themselves? Or was it others, possibly pejoratively?
