In his books and lectures, Manfred Spitzer explains that people do not remember the grammar of their native language through individual cases, but through general (unconscious) rules. To demonstrate this, he has readers and listeners put imaginary words into the past participle, namely a two-syllable word such as “quaffen” and a three-syllable word ending in “-ieren,” such as “flagieren.” He then claims that the result, “gequafft” and “flagiert,” means that words ending in “-ieren” form an exception. But I think this has nothing to do with “-ieren,” and instead purely with the fact that it is a three-syllable word without a prefix. To check this suspicion, however, I would need more genuinely three-syllable verbs, which are rare in German. My question is now: am I right in my suspicion about three-syllable words? Can anyone give me a few examples of genuinely three-syllable verbs that support or refute it? Or is this even stated as a grammar rule in a good grammar book? Or does the explanation in terms of “-ieren” have a (good or bad) tradition?
