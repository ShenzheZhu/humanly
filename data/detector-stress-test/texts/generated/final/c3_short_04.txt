Interested by an earlier question about pronouns, I was wondering why lui can be the subject in some cases, but not moi (I am speaking here of noun phrases made up of a single word). (I had a terrible time finding a quotation that was not biblical, because religious texts make abundant use of “lui.”) We realize that the same effect in the first and second persons requires a repetition of the subject: “for moi, je ne pleurais pas” (“for me, I was not crying”). In the plural, the repetition is also necessary to distinguish them from the usual nous and vous: “nous, nous ne pleurions pas” (“we, we were not crying”). What can explain why lui and eux can avoid the repetition, unlike moi, toi, nous, and vous?
