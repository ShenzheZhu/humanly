# On moral decline, cultural suicide and the Norwegian “integration tragedy,” etc.

I will not exactly deny that it was with a certain skepticism that I decided to read this book. The reason lies in some of the media statements that have come from the strongly media-focused historian of religion Hanne Nabintu Herland over the last few years. Quite a lot of her messages are so sharpened that they are more suited to provoking than to actually forming the basis for good and nuanced public debates. I do, however, understand very well that the media love to bring forward a commentator who is completely fearless and who can take the pressure when the storm is at its worst. And who does not shy away from giving the media what the media want — namely provocative and hard-hitting headlines in the news picture. And one must give her that — she is and remains an amusing breath of fresh air!

Herland believes that it is time to raise respect for Norwegian culture, and that there is no contradiction between holding on to one’s own cultural identity and living in ethnic diversity. Not least, she believes that the USA shows that this is possible. In interviews about the book, she has among other things stated that in “Alarm!” she makes a total reckoning with the culture-radical 68ers’ way of thinking. What she is referring to is the downgrading of one’s own ideals and values that has taken place for Europeans in general in the encounter with non-Western immigrants. She believes that today it is looked down upon if one highlights the great ideals and values that have made Europe a great civilization. The cultural suicide that we are in the process of committing in Europe, Herland believes, must stop. The fact that we are inclined to feel sorry for the immigrants who come here she characterizes as a form of racism.

So far, basically all well and good ... But at some point in the book, in my view, it tipped over so much that I had problems keeping up with the turns ... And I became so furious that I had to mobilize the strength to bother reading on. Some of what I reacted to most was outright personal attacks on named persons, and this after the author herself had criticized left-wing radicals for making personal attacks on named value-conservative people in society. In other words, she does precisely what she accuses others of doing. For example, I experienced the attack on the relationship expert Thue as ugly. I did not recognize the attitudes she attributed to him, and perceived her explanations as rather condescending.

Besides, it does not entirely seem as if Herland understands how laws are made in Norwegian society, that is, that these are passed by the Storting and not by whatever government is sitting at the time. One example of this is the church’s lack of follow-up of the Partnership Act, that is, some priests’ resistance to marrying homosexuals. Herland is horrified that politicians even try to force their values on the church in a secularized society, and believes that religion should be elevated above the secular. This is a line of reasoning I have no problem following when it comes to confessions of faith and/or church questions of a more theological nature. But that the church should be elevated above ordinary legislation that is meant to ensure that, for example, discrimination does not take place — there I drop off. Without interference from legislation, we would never have had female priests today, and everyone knows what a struggle it has been for homosexuals to be allowed to practice within this profession.

Furthermore, I think Herland goes rather far when she compares Norwegian society with the former Soviet Union, because she believes that people who think differently are punished so harshly. OK, I take the point — but at any rate, one does not kill people who think differently in a country like Norway, and one also does not risk a life sentence for having deviant opinions. If a public official, or someone who is on the public payroll, is not willing to follow the laws and regulations that apply to that person’s professional practice — well, then one must actually accept that a consequence may be that one loses one’s job ... without Norway thereby being characterized almost as a totalitarian society! I am almost inclined to state that it really is not possible to claim such a thing!

That neither Norway nor other countries in Europe have succeeded very well in integrating quite a few of the non-Western immigrants is, moreover, not difficult to agree with. Herland also has a point when she emphasizes that there have been some costs when we have privatized religion, because we have lost important society-building values along the way. One would think that European society was particularly suited to increasing people’s quality of life, because we have never had greater freedom, more material prosperity and more choices, but in fact it is to a large extent the opposite that has happened. It is enough to mention the number of suicides, especially among young men, drug abuse and loneliness among very many people.

Unlike Herland, however, I am optimistic about the future in a multicultural Norway. Perhaps another generational shift or two is needed before we are completely at the goal, but the way the very young socialize in a multicultural society is truly an example to follow! That in the meantime we must quota immigrants into working life is one of several measures that are needed, and this is not about feeling sorry for dark-skinned people. It is instead about breaking down existing prejudices among Norwegians through legislation! Just think how outrageous the business community found the requirement of 40% female participation on boards and councils! Do we really believe that there is not a sufficient number of competent women in Norwegian society? In a transitional phase one tried voluntariness, but then, unfortunately, sadly little happened. Legal regulation is actually necessary to reach such goals — and this is no different for immigrants, who experience that despite higher education they do not even get interviews.

Otherwise, I think Herland ought to limit herself to being a national pundit on what she undoubtedly knows a lot about, namely the history of religion, and be somewhat more cautious about coming up with too categorical opinions on socio-economic matters. Some of the claims about Norway’s oil money and the use of it are actually directly wrong. This disfigures an otherwise provocative book that is absolutely a fresh contribution to the public debate, where she obviously dares to put into words a number of conditions it is not entirely acceptable to speak loudly about. I do not entirely care for her style or the way she does this. And perhaps some of my antipathy is due to the fact that we have such totally different political standpoints. On the other hand, more voices are needed in the debate, and I respect the fact that she does not have the same opinions as I do! Finally, an important clarification: Herland is not a researcher, even if the media are fond of calling her that.
