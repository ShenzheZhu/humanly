Overview

This book chapter overviews what motivates different types of mindsets. From reading this chapter, you should gain knowledge of mindsets, including the definition, types of mindsets, causes of the types of mindsets, and further related ideas. From there, this chapter will emphasise what motivates people to have the type of mindset they possess, and how to alter it.

What is a mindset?

“When you are faced with a challenging task, do you (a) complete the task, or (b) watch a TV show? Your answer to this is determined by your mindset” (Steverink & Lindenberg, 2006).

A mindset is a frame of mind, or the beliefs, thoughts, or series of self-perceptions humans possess (De Young, 1986). This can determine an individual’s situation, mental attitude, and behaviour. For example, a mindset of “I am not good at playing the piano” will result in the behaviour of not playing the piano. However, a mindset of “I haven’t learnt how to play the piano properly yet” is more likely to result in practising the piano until the individual is good at playing it (Krakovsky, 2008).

The term mindset was first discovered by Stanford University psychologist Carol Dweck. Dweck argues that individuals obtain their abilities from a variety of beliefs they hold (Mercer & Ryan, 2009). Dweck found these beliefs to be centred around either a fixed mindset or a growth mindset (Krakovsky, 2008).

Fixed mindset

A fixed mindset is where individuals believe qualities such as talent or intelligence are qualities they are born with. This fixed mindset leads individuals to view their intelligence and talents as fixed, and to spend less time developing such qualities (Cecere, Mancinelli, S & Mazzanti, 2014). A person with this type of mindset believes that effort is not required to pursue goals or be successful, as the talent and intelligence they were born with create either success or failure (Mercer & Ryan, 2009).

People with a fixed mindset typically describe themselves as “intelligent” or “dumb,” and will not do anything to change these abilities (Govindarajan & Gupta, 2009). When these people are faced with a challenge or failure, they tell others and themselves that “they are unable to accomplish it,” or create other excuses to explain their failure (e.g., “I failed the exam because I was busy studying for another unit and didn’t have enough time to study for this exam”). This is because their egos are easily threatened, and they have a need to prove themselves (Cecere, Mancinelli, S & Mazzanti, 2014).
