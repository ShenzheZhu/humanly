# A gem of a book!

It is beginning to be some years since I last read Emile Ajar’s lovely novel *The Life Before Us*.

Now the book has appeared in a new edition from the publisher Agora Publishing (with the same translation as the original Norwegian edition from 1976 — Anne Elligers’s), which, in cooperation with Aschehoug Publishing, aims to “publish some of the most significant foreign writers of our time in Norwegian translation,” “... most of them ... already well established on the international literary scene,” to quote from an article about the launch of the publishing house on Aschehoug’s website. Agora Library, in other words, consists of a selection of exquisite literature, and it is the writer Gabi Gleichmann who is the editor of the editions.

The man behind the pseudonym Emile Ajar is Romain Gary (b. 1914 d. 1980). He published quite a number of books during his writing career — about 40. There is a rather special story behind his choice to publish some books under the pseudonym Emile Ajar. Allegedly, he was tired of himself, and through this pseudonym got the opportunity to be a spectator to his other life. That it was he who was behind the novel *The Life Before Us* did not become known until some time after he had won the Goncourt Prize for the book, more precisely after he had taken his own life in 1980. He was originally from Lithuania and was not even named Romain Gary, but Roman Kacew, and was of Jewish descent.

In *The Life Before Us* we meet the orphan boy Momo, whose real name is Mohammed. He does not know where he comes from, and he does not know how old he really is. The only thing he actually knows is that he is of Arab descent, because Madame Rosa, with whom he lives, insists that he needs a little Muslim upbringing. Madame Rosa has a past as a prostitute, and is herself Jewish — one of those who survived the Nazis’ extermination of the Jews during the war. Now she is getting old and frail, and we sense the beginning of a rapidly advancing dementia. She is no longer able to take proper care of herself, much less the children who have been placed with her by their prostitute mothers, who otherwise risk losing their children to the Welfare authorities. What might happen then is associated with horror and dread, without the children really quite grasping exactly what it is about.

Momo is the narrator and the “I” of the book. Madame Rosa claims that he is 10 years old, but later it turns out that he is 14 and that she has held back his age because she is afraid of losing him, her dearest child. The environment they are part of consists of prostitutes, transsexuals, and poor immigrants, mainly from African countries.

Momo looks at the world with his child’s eyes and his own unbeatable logic. Many of the observations he makes are so funny that I howled with laughter along the way. With statements such as “As long as there is life, there is no peace to be had” (page 18), “She puts on quite a lot of makeup, but it is no use playing hide-and-seek at her age” (page 20), “It is completely ridiculous nowadays, now that all that is dead and buried, but Jews are impossible to get rid of, especially when they have been exterminated, they keep coming back to it” (page 36), and “I remember I said it straight out to her, you have to slim down in order to eat less, but it is terribly hard for an old person who is alone in the world. She needed more of herself. When you are not surrounded by love, it turns into fat” (page 51) — well, you just have to surrender completely.

And I continue on **page 108**:

> “In Africa they stick together in tribes where old people are very much in demand because of everything they can do for you when they are dead. In France there are no tribes because of selfishness. Monsieur Walumba says that France has become completely de-tribed, and that is why there are armed gangs who stay faithfully together and try to accomplish something. Monsieur Walumba says that young people need a tribe, because otherwise they become like drops in the ocean, and that drives them completely crazy. Monsieur Walumba says that everything is getting so big these days that there is no point in even counting until you get to a thousand. That is why old people, who cannot join together in armed gangs in order to exist, disappear without leaving an address and live hidden away in dusty corners. No one knows about them, especially in attic rooms without elevators, when they cannot shout to say that there is someone there, because they do not have the strength for that. Monsieur Walumba says that large forces of manpower ought to be sent in from Africa to look for old people every morning at six o’clock and drive away the ones who are beginning to smell, because no one comes to check whether the old person is still alive, and it is only when someone says to the caretaker that there is a smell in the stairwell that everything is explained.”

Screamingly funny, while at the same time there is a certain sadness over it all, because what little Momo is describing is poverty and loneliness in all their horror ... One does not need much imagination to understand what he is really talking about. And it is precisely the alternation between the humorous and the serious that is this novel’s strength, while Momo’s unbeatable logic runs through the book like a red thread. He is so brainwashed into doing everything in his power to avoid the Welfare authorities that even on the day when he actually has the chance to get away from a Madame Rosa who is no longer at her best — even then he does not seize the opportunity ...

When I received a reading copy of the book from the publisher, an article about the author — written by Gabi Gleichmann — came along with it. Here Gleichmann writes the following:

> “A world of drug abuse, racism, prostitution, misery, and segregation is described with immense insight. Thirty-five years after the book’s publication, its picture of life at the bottom of French society is more relevant than ever.

> The novel enjoyed enormous success. It is in fact the best-selling and most widely read book in the history of France. When it was crowned with the country’s highest literary distinction, the Goncourt Prize, it triggered wild speculation about who was hiding behind the name Emile Ajar. No one doubted that it had to be an important writer. Many great names were suggested. Gary too. But the critics dismissed this possibility with the argument: ‘Oh no, Gary does not have sufficient talent.’”

*The Life Before Us* is a fantastic novel in every way! The narrative style and the voice of the young boy Momo, who turns out to be somewhat older than Madame Rosa has told him, are carried through to the very tips of the fingers. The depiction of an environment consisting of people at the very bottom of the social ladder is solid and authentic, and here a solidarity is portrayed so strong that no one allows anyone to sink all the way to the bottom without reaching out a helping hand. It is precisely this solidarity that is described so touchingly that tears pressed on a few times. If it had not been for the fact that Agora Publishing’s edition of the book is so beautiful, with a solid binding one does not often see these days, I would have taken out my yellow highlighter and underlined the most wonderful passages in the book while reading it. But that was exactly what I did not feel like doing in this splendid copy of a book.

This is a book I am going to read several times before I die. And since I am on that subject, I must say that I wonder why the book has not been included in *1001 Books You Must Read Before You Die*. It would certainly have deserved a place there! Otherwise, I am very pleased that this book is now available to new readers! May more people open their eyes to this treasure of a book!
