The paper proposes an attention-based approach for video description. The approach uses three LSTMs and two attention mechanisms to sequentially predict words from a sequence of frames.

In the LSTM encoder of the frames (TEM), the first attention mechanism predicts spatial attention per frame and computes a weighted average. The second LSTM (HAM) predicts attention over the hidden states of the encoder LSTM.

The third LSTM, which runs temporally in parallel with the second LSTM, generates the sentence one word at a time.

Strengths:
===============

- The paper addresses a relevant and interesting problem.
- Using two layers of attention in the proposed way has, to my knowledge, not been used before for video description. The exact architecture is thus novel, but the work claims much more without sufficient attribution; see below.
- The experiments are evaluated on two datasets, MSVD and Charades, showing performance on the level of related work for MSVD and improvements for Charades.

Weaknesses:
===============

1. Claims about the contribution and novelty of the model do not seem to hold.

1.1. One of the main contributions is the Hierarchical Attention/Memory (HAM):

1.1.1. It is not clear to me how the presented model (Eq. 6-8) is significantly different from the models in Xu et al. and Yao et al. While Xu et al. attend over spatial image locations and Yao et al. attend over frames, this model attends over encoded video representations h_v^i. A slight difference might be that Xu et al. use the same LSTM to generate, while this model uses an additional LSTM for decoding.

1.1.2. The paper states in Section 3.2, “we propose f_m to memorize the previous attention.” However, H_m^{t’-1} consists only of the last hidden state. Furthermore, the model f_m does not have access to the attention \alpha. This was also discussed in comments by others but remains unclear.

1.1.3. In the discussion of comments, the authors claim that “attention not only is a function a current time step but also a function of all previous attentions and network states.” While it is true that there is a dependency, this is also true for any LSTM. However, the model does not have access to the previous network states, since H_g^{t’-1} consists only of the last hidden state, as does H_m^{t’-1}—at least that is what the formulas say and what Figure 1 suggests.

1.1.4. The authors claim to have multi-layer attention in HAM, but it remains unclear where the multi-layer aspect comes from.

1.2. The paper states in Section 3.1 that “[CNN] features tend to discard the low level information useful in modeling the motion in the video (Ballas et al., 2016).” This suggests that the approach that follows addresses this problem. However, it cannot model motion, as attention \rho between frames is not available when predicting the next frame. Also, it is not clear how the model can capture anything “low level,” since it operates on rather high-level VGG conv 5 features.

2. Related work: The difference between HAM and the models of Yao et al. and Xu et al. should be made clearer, or these papers should be cited in the HAM section.

3. Conceptual limitation of the model: The model has two independent attention mechanisms, a spatial one and a temporal one. The spatial attention within a frame is independent of sentence generation. It therefore cannot attend to different aspects of the frames for different words, which would make sense. For example, if the sentence is “the dog jumps on the trampoline,” the model should focus on the dog when saying “dog” and on the trampoline when saying “trampoline.” However, since the spatial attention is fixed, this is difficult. Also, the encoder model does not have an explicit way to look at different aspects of the frame during encoding, so it might likely get stuck and always predict the same spatial attention for all frames. Alternatively, it might always attend to the dog as it moves around, but never to the scene.

4. Eq. 11 contradicts Fig. 1: How exactly is the model receiving the previous word as input? Eq. 11 suggests that it is the softmax. If this is the case, the authors should emphasize this in the text, since it is unusual. More common would be to use the ground-truth previous word during training, which Fig. 1 suggests, and the “hardmax,” i.e., the highest predicted previous word encoded as a one-hot vector, at test time.

5. Clarity:

5.1. It would be helpful if the same notation were used in Eq. 2-5 and Eq. 6-9. Why is different notation required?

5.2. It would be helpful if Fig. 1 contained more details, or if additional figures for the corresponding parts were added. If space is a problem, the well-known equations for LSTM, softmax (Eq. 2), and log-likelihood loss (Eq. 12) could be omitted or inlined.

6. Evaluation:

6.1. The paper claims that “the proposed architecture outperforms all previously proposed methods and leads to a new state of the art results.”

6.1.1. For the MSVD dataset, this is clearly wrong, even given the same feature representation. Pan et al. (2016a) achieve higher METEOR (33.10) in Table 2.

6.1.2. For this strong claim, I would also expect the model to outperform all previous results independent of the features used, which is also wrong. Yu et al. achieve higher performance in all compared metrics.

6.1.3. For the Charades dataset, this claim is also too bold, since hardly any methods have been evaluated on this dataset. At least all ablations reported in Table 1 should also be reported for the Charades dataset to support any stronger claims for this dataset.

6.2. Missing qualitative results of attention: The authors should show qualitative results for both attention mechanisms to understand whether anything sensible is happening there. How diverse are the spatial and temporal attentions? Are they peaky or rather uniform?

6.3. Performance improvement is not significant over model ablations: The improvement over Att+No TEM is only 0.5 METEOR and 0.7 BLEU@4, and the performance drops for CIDEr by 1.7.

6.4. Missing human evaluation: I disagree with the authors that a human evaluation is not feasible. First, an evaluation on a subset of the test data is not so difficult. Second, even if other authors do not provide their code or model, and some do, they are typically happy to share the predicted sentences, which is sufficient and even better for human evaluation. If not, I would explicitly mention that some authors did not share sentences, as this seems clearly wrong. Third, for model ablations, the sentences are available to the authors.

7. Several of the comments raised by reviewers or others have not yet been incorporated in a revised version of the paper and/or are still not clear from the explanations given. For example, including SPICE evaluation and making fixes seems trivial.

8. Hyperparameters are inconsistent: Why are the hyperparameters inconsistent between the ablation analysis, where 40 frames are sampled, and the performance comparison, where 8 frames are sampled? Should this not be selected on the validation set? What is the performance of all the ablations with 8 frames?

Other minor/discussion points:
===============

- Equation 10: What happens with h_m and h_g? The LSTM formulas provided only handle two inputs. Are h_m and h_g concatenated?
- There is a Section 4.1 but no Section 4.2.
- The paper states in Section 4.1, “our proposed architecture can alone not only learn a representation for video that can model the temporal structure of a video sequence, but also a representation that can effectively map visual space to the language space.” However, this seems to be true also for many or most other approaches, e.g., Venugopalan et al. 2015 ICCV.

Summary:
===============

While the paper makes strong claims with respect to the approach and results, the approach lacks novelty, and the results are not convincing compared with related work and ablations. Furthermore, improved clarity and visualizations of the model and attention results would benefit the paper.
