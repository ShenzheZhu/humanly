It is the linguistic skill that refers to the interpretation of oral discourse. It is worth remembering that a skill is a way of “using” language and that it is related to the mode of transmission, oral or written, and that, according to the role it plays in communication, it may be receptive or productive in type.

All comprehension, whether listening or reading, is a cognitive process, since the listener/reader relates what he or she already knows or is familiar with to what he or she hears/reads in order to validate or modify a previous approximate “idea”; an idea that had been formed about the new content on the basis of the situation, the topic, etc.

In other words: once physiological, environmental, and code-related issues have been overcome (that is, if there are no problems in the hearing/vision system, if the volume and intensity of the voice are satisfactory/if there is enough light in the case of reading, and if the person has a minimum experience of the language), there will be comprehension if there is confirmation or viability between the previous idea and the new information or, if such confirmation does not exist, there will be comprehension if one proceeds to listen/read again, looking once more for validation or for an attempt to revise the idea in order to move on to an alternative idea.

Synthetic processing of information

When listening, first the noises and non-linguistic sounds that we hear constantly are set aside, so as to pay attention only to the sounds of speech (the brain works here at the phonic level). Then these sounds are identified as phonemes (phonological level) that form words (lexical level), which in turn form phrases (activation of the morphological level), and with the help of morphosyntactic relations, which are the ones that, for example, regulate word order, meaning is given to these phrases (semantic level), and phrase by phrase a coherent text is formed, which is interpreted thanks to or according to the situation (pragmatic level).

Analytical processing of information

When listening, the first things that are understood are the most general aspects of the discourse (the type of text, the communicative situation, the general idea, what is specific to each paragraph), and only afterward are the linguistic units such as phrase or word heard/understood.

The segmentation of the acoustic chain is a micro-skill in which, as in all the others, it is advisable to train students, in order to accustom them to identifying a word within a more extensive series of sounds (identifying a known piece of data within a series of unknown data).
