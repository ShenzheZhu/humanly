Last year I self-published a short nonfiction book about spending a year systematically training in table tennis and trying to improve from a complete amateur. Because the subject was so niche, I did not have high expectations. But after it went online, it stayed near the top of the relevant Amazon categories for a long time, and for several weeks it was number one. Many friends saw the ranking and assumed I must have made a fortune.

The reality is that a good ranking does not mean astonishing sales. So far, the paperback, ebook, and audiobook combined have sold only around a thousand copies. After platform fees, cover design, proofreading, and recording costs, what I actually kept was probably a few thousand dollars. It was not a loss, but it was far from the life of a “bestselling author.”

I feel conflicted. On one hand, I am genuinely happy when strangers tell me the book made them pick up a paddle again. On the other, I clearly overestimated the market for niche nonfiction. Now I am wondering whether, if I keep doing similar projects, I should first build an audience, or simply accept that this can only be a long-term side project.
