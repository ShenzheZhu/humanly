For most people, the tool in the figure below is simply a shovel, as it was for me before answering this question about the Portuguese equivalent of spade and shovel. But in the trade this type of shovel is known as a (pá) vanga or pá de corte, as can be seen on this seller’s website, source of the figure below. The difference is that ordinary shovels are designed to lift and carry or throw material, so, besides being wider, they are either curved or have raised edges to hold it; the vanga, by contrast, is designed for digging, and is therefore narrower and straight, to penetrate and cut the soil easily. General dictionaries do not list vanga; they list pá direita (Aulete) with the same meaning, but this term does not seem to be used. I found vanga in some specialized dictionaries, which, however, do not explain where the term comes from: pá de corte is perfectly understandable: this type of shovel cuts the soil. But where does vanga come from, and when did it start being used?
