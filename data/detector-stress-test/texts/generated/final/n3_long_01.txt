This paper studies the problem of unsupervised next-frame or multi-frame video prediction. Its central claim is that future frames should not be regressed directly in pixel space; instead, motion should be modeled in the space of local affine transformations between adjacent frames. Specifically, the method first divides video frames into overlapping patches and estimates, for each patch, the affine transformation from the current frame to the next frame. It then trains a convolutional network that takes affine transformation fields from several past time steps as input and predicts future affine transformation fields. At test time, the predicted transformations are applied to image patches from the previous frame, and the generated frame is obtained by averaging over overlapping regions. The authors also propose an evaluation protocol for video generation: an action classifier is trained on real videos, and the generated video frames are then fed into this classifier; classification accuracy is used to measure whether the generated sequence preserves discriminative information useful for downstream recognition tasks. Experiments are mainly conducted on moving MNIST and UCF-101. The results show that the method outperforms several baselines in visual sharpness, parameter count, and classifier-based evaluation metrics, including copying the last frame, optical-flow extrapolation, and a more complex multi-scale adversarial video prediction model.

In my view, the main strength of the paper is that it approaches the problem from a clear and practically meaningful angle. In video prediction, using pixel-level MSE often leads to blur, which is a well-known but still difficult problem. The authors propose using MSE in transformation space rather than pixel space. Intuitively, this shifts the uncertainty from “which pixel appearance should be generated” to “which motion should be chosen,” thereby avoiding the averaging of pixels over multiple possible futures. Although this idea is related to optical flow, motion-compensated video coding, spatial transformer networks, and similar areas, it is valuable as a simple, trainable, and comparable baseline within a deep video prediction framework. In particular, the paper emphasizes local affine transformations rather than directly predicting dense pixels or the entire frame, which keeps the model small, reduces computation, and makes it easier for generated results to preserve sharp edges and textures.

A second strength is that the method has a relatively simple structure and is therefore fairly interpretable. The responsibilities of the affine transformation extractor, the affine transformation predictor, and the transformation applicator are clearly separated. The paper also handles the training procedure for multi-step prediction in a reasonable way: instead of training only one-step prediction and then applying it recursively at test time, it unrolls multiple time steps during training while sharing parameters, exposing the model to the state distribution produced by its own predictions. This is very important for video prediction, because purely one-step training often leads to severe error accumulation. The authors also provide ablation experiments on the number of input frames, the number of prediction steps, and the patch size, showing that the model is not extremely sensitive to some hyperparameters.

A third commendable aspect is that the authors recognize the inadequacy of traditional pixel MSE for evaluating video generation and attempt to propose an evaluation protocol more closely related to semantics and the preservation of discriminative information. For natural videos, the future is often not unique, and a generated result that differs from the ground-truth frame pixel by pixel is not necessarily of poor quality. Using an action classifier trained on real videos to evaluate whether generated videos still contain action-category information is an insightful direction. Compared with merely reporting MSE or displaying subjective examples, this evaluation at least attempts to quantify the usefulness of generated sequences for subsequent tasks, and to some extent avoids over-penalizing plausible future motions that differ from the ground truth.

However, the paper also has several fairly obvious problems. First, the novelty of the method needs to be positioned more carefully. Local motion estimation, block-based affine compensation, optical-flow extrapolation, and motion-compensated reconstruction all have long histories in video processing. The learning component of this paper mainly trains a CNN to predict future parameters from already estimated local affine parameters, rather than learning an end-to-end generative model from pixels to future frames. This design is reasonable in itself, but the paper needs to distinguish more clearly its contribution from traditional motion compensation, optical-flow prediction, dynamic texture modeling, and spatial-transformation-based models. The current related discussion is rather brief, which can make the method seem like a combination of classical block matching or local transformation estimation with a small CNN. If the authors wish to emphasize that this is a strong baseline, they should demonstrate this more systematically in both experiments and discussion, rather than comparing only with a small number of deep models.

Second, the details and cost of the affine transformation extraction step are not sufficiently clear. The paper says that the affine transformations of all patches are jointly estimated by minimizing the whole-frame reconstruction error, but it does not adequately explain the optimization process, initialization method, constraints, convergence criteria, failure cases, or running time. Since the entire subsequent model depends on these “pseudo-label” affine parameters, the quality of the extractor is crucial to the final results. If this step itself is computationally expensive or sensitive to hyperparameters, then the claim that the prediction model has few parameters and generation is cheap is incomplete, because the preprocessing cost of the training data has not been fully counted. In addition, local affine transformations are often ill-posed in textureless regions, occluded regions, motion boundaries, and areas where new content appears. The paper does not sufficiently analyze whether the estimated transformations are stable in these cases, or how these noisy labels affect the prediction network.

Third, the reconstruction mechanism itself has clear limitations in expressive power. The method can essentially generate future frames only by warping existing pixels, so it has difficulty handling newly appearing object regions, background revealed after occlusion, non-rigid deformation, illumination changes, large scale changes, and complex three-dimensional motion. The authors acknowledge some of these limitations, but the experimental analysis is still insufficient. Many short-term actions in UCF-101 can be explained by approximate translation or small deformation, so it is not entirely surprising that the method performs well when predicting 4 to 8 frames. The paper needs to show failure modes under longer time horizons, larger motion, stronger occlusion, and more complex camera motion. Otherwise, it is hard for readers to judge whether the method is a general video prediction model or mainly suitable for short-term motion compensation.

Fourth, although the evaluation protocol is creative, it has several potential biases. Measuring generation quality by action-classifier accuracy actually evaluates only whether the generated sequence preserves the discriminative features relied on by the classifier, not necessarily visual realism, temporal consistency, or the plausibility of future prediction. The classifier may depend mainly on the appearance or background cues in the first few frames rather than on the motion information in the generated frames. The classification accuracies for real 4-frame and real 8-frame clips in the paper are almost the same, suggesting that the marginal contribution of additional temporal information may be small. In that case, the quality of the generated frames may have limited influence on classification accuracy. Copying the last frame also achieves fairly high accuracy, further indicating that this metric may be easy to support with static appearance cues. Therefore, although this metric is more semantically meaningful than pixel MSE, it cannot by itself serve as sufficient evidence of video generation quality. The authors should add more evaluations, such as human preference tests, perceptual distances, temporal consistency metrics, optical-flow consistency, edge-sharpness statistics, or at least analyze the classifier’s sensitivity under settings such as masking the background, using only generated frames, or excluding the conditioning frames.

Fifth, the fairness and completeness of the experimental comparisons still need improvement. The comparison with the optical-flow baseline appears to use very simple constant-flow extrapolation, whereas the optical-flow literature contains stronger methods for handling motion boundaries, hierarchical estimation, and robust extrapolation. The comparison with the multi-scale adversarial video prediction model also requires more training details, including whether it was trained on the same data, at the same resolution, with the same number of input frames, the same number of prediction steps, and the same training budget, and whether that model was sufficiently tuned. The paper claims substantially lower computation and parameter count, which is an important advantage, but if the comparison models were not optimized under the current dataset and evaluation protocol, the conclusion is weakened. In addition, the moving MNIST section provides only qualitative results and no quantitative comparison. Although the UCF-101 section reports classification accuracy, it lacks confidence intervals or variance over multiple runs. Some gaps between methods in the table are less than one percentage point, so their statistical significance is unclear.

I am also concerned that using MSE to predict affine parameters may still produce an “average motion” problem; the problem is merely transferred from pixel space to motion space. The authors also observe that motion magnitude is underestimated. For multimodal futures, the average affine transformation may not correspond to any real motion, especially near motion boundaries, where a patch may contain two objects or a mixture of foreground and background. The averaged transformation may then cause incorrect warping. The paper suggests modeling transformation distributions or using adversarial training in the future, but the current method does not solve this key issue. Therefore, the paper’s claim that “MSE in transformation space does not produce blur” should be stated more modestly: it can indeed preserve the local sharpness of warped pixels, but it may produce insufficient motion, ghosting, local misalignment, or unrealistic dynamics.

I would like the authors to answer several specific questions. Is the affine transformation extractor optimized independently for each pair of frames, or does it use temporal smoothing or shared constraints? How long does optimization take for each pair of video frames, and what is the total preprocessing cost for the training set? In textureless or occluded regions, do affine parameters drift arbitrarily, and are regularization or boundary constraints used? When generating multiple frames at test time, if a transformation samples pixels outside the image, or if a target region is not covered by any source pixels, how exactly is it filled? In the classifier evaluation, is the input to the classifier a clip containing only generated frames, or a mixture of conditioning frames and generated frames? If the classifier sees only the conditioning frames, what is the accuracy? This is crucial for judging whether the generated frames truly contribute action information. Have the authors tried directly predicting optical flow rather than 6-parameter affine block transformations? Such a comparison would help clarify the advantage of the affine patch representation over dense optical flow. Finally, can the authors provide longer prediction horizons, such as 16 or 32 frames, and results broken down by action category, to reveal which types of motion suit the model and which types fail?

Overall, my evaluation of the paper is positive but with reservations. The paper proposes a simple, effective, and computationally efficient method for short-term video prediction, and it emphasizes that modeling in transformation space can alleviate the blur caused by pixel MSE, which is practically valuable. The generated results and classifier-based evaluation on UCF-101 show that the method can serve as a strong and concise baseline, offering a useful reference for later video prediction research. However, the paper is still incomplete in its positioning of novelty, details of affine extraction, adequacy of evaluation metrics, fairness of experiments, and analysis of failure modes. If this were a conference submission, I would lean toward weak acceptance: the contribution is not revolutionary, and the evaluation protocol needs more rigorous validation, but the method is clear and the results are persuasive in certain respects. In particular, achieving better performance than more complex baselines with a very small model deserves the community’s attention. I suggest that the authors substantially strengthen the experimental details in the final version, add more comprehensive evaluations, and discuss more explicitly the scope and limitations of the method as a short-term, motion-compensation-style video prediction baseline.
