*** Paper Summary ***

The paper proposes a new neural network architecture. The layer weights of a classical network are computed as a function of a latent representation associated with the layer. Two instances are presented: (i) a CNN where each layer weight is computed from a lower-dimensional layer-embedding vector; and (ii) an RNN where each layer weight is computed from a secondary RNN state.

*** Review Summary ***

Pros:
- I like the idea of bringing multiplicative RNNs and their predecessors back into the spotlight.
- The LM and MT results are excellent.

Cons:
- The paper could be better written. It is too long for the conference format and needs refocusing.
- In the related work, the relation to multiplicative RNNs and their generic tensor-product predecessors (Order 2 networks, with respect to C. Lee Giles's definition) should be mentioned, and the differences from earlier research need to be explained and motivated. By the way, it is better to say that something is revisiting an old idea or training it at modern scale/on modern tasks than omitting it.
- In terms of focus, it is not clear whether your goal is to achieve better performance or more compact networks. In the RNN section you lean toward the former; in the CNN section you seem to lean toward the latter.

I would suggest making the paper shorter and clearer, possibly leaving the CNN results for later publication. The relation to multiplicative/Order 2 networks and any differences need to be explained.

*** Detailed Review ***

Multiplicative networks are an extremely powerful architecture, and bringing them back into the spotlight is excellent. This paper has excellent results but suffers from poor presentation and a lack of clear focus. It spends time on details and omits important points. In its current form, it is much too long and is not self-contained without the appendices.

Spending more time on multiplicative RNNs and Order 2 networks at the beginning of the paper would be excellent. This would let you highlight the difference between this paper and earlier work. It would also be necessary to spend a little time on why multiplicative RNNs were used less than gated RNNs: it seems that the optimization problem involved in their training is trickier, and it would be helpful to explain whether you had a harder time tweaking optimization parameters or whether you needed longer training sessions compared to LSTMs and regular CNNs. On the name, I am not sure that "hypernetwork" helps the reader understand the proposed architecture better than multiplicative interactions.

In Section 3.2, you seem to imply that there are different settings of hypernetworks that allow one to vary from an RNN to a CNN. This is not clear to me. Maybe you could show how this would work on a simple temporal problem with equations.

The work on CNNs and RNNs seems rather disconnected to me. For CNNs, you seem to be interested in a low-rank structure of the weights, showing that similar performance can be achieved with fewer weights. It is not clear to me why one should pursue that goal. Do you expect speedups? Less memory use for embedded applications? In that case, you should compare with alternative strategies, e.g., model compression (Caruana et al. 2006, aka Dark Knowledge, Hinton et al. 2014) or hashed networks (Chen et al. 2015).

For RNNs, you seem to target better perplexity/BLEU, and model compactness is not a priority. Instead of making the weights have a simpler structure, you make them richer, i.e., dependent over time. It seems that in this case models might be bigger and take longer to train. You might want to comment on training time, inference time, and memory requirements in that case, since you highlight that this might be an important goal in the CNN section. Overall, I am not sure it helps to have this mixed message. I would rather see the paper fit the conference format with the RNN results alone and a clearer explanation, deferring publication of the CNN results until a proper comparison with memory-concerned methods is performed.

Some of the discussions are not clear to me. I am not sure what message the reader should get from Figure 2 or from the discussion on saturation statistics (p. 10, Figure 5). Similarly, I am not sure whether Figure 4 is showing anything: everything should change more drastically at word boundaries even in a regular LSTM (states and gate units should look very different before and after a space). Without such a comparison, it is hard to see whether this is unique to your network.

The results on handwriting generation are harder for me to compare. Log-loss is hard to understand, and I have no sense of whether the difference between models is significant. What would be the variance in this metric under bootstrap sampling of the training set? I am also not sold on a qualitative metric where humans can assess quality but cannot evaluate whether the network is repeating the training set. Did you think about a precision/recall metric for ink, possibly with some spatial tolerance? For example, this could be similar to the evaluation of segmentation tasks in vision.

The MT experiments are insufficiently discussed in the main text.

Overall, I would suggest making the paper shorter and clearer, possibly leaving the CNN results for later publication. You need to properly discuss the relation to multiplicative/Order 2 networks and highlight the differences. Unclear discussion can be eliminated to make the experimental setup and results presentation clearer in the main text.
