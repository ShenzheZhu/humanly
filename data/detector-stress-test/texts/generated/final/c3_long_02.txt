One often has different angles of approach to a book. Mine was strong curiosity. Because why would my youngest daughter ask me—who have over 4,000 books in my library—whether I had read this one? And: she had just returned home from a year’s trip in Australia as a backpacker. So I ordered it. And I became even more curious because I received a copy published by Bazaar, because the book had its 20-year anniversary. And in his foreword—to this “special” edition—he says that he was sitting on Copacabana with blank pages in front of him, about to begin his—always difficult—book number 2. He is sitting there and sees a fisherman pulling up his anchors, and it strikes him: Santiago! We who have read Hemingway’s “The Old Man and the Sea” (which Coelho has also done) know that the old fisherman was called Santiago. And with that he has a beginning: He writes: The boy’s name was Santiago. And then he was underway.

Now I do not know whether he is already conscious here of the mysticism in a name. After reading the book, I would think so. How many cities there are called Santiago I do not know. Perhaps the best known is the capital of Chile. But in Spanish-speaking names (especially) it is a much-used male name. It comes from Jacob, of whom there are two who are known in the Bible; the first is in the Old Testament, and the second is St. James—Jesus’s brother, whom I believe pilgrims (people who travel with a GOAL in mind) use as their “logo.” (?)

And then there is this business of Maktub, which he places great emphasis on. Why? He himself says it can be translated as “It is written,” but you have to be born an Arab to understand it. Well, I asked someone who was born an Arab, and quite right; it was not only that which has already been written (as history), but that which was always meant to happen to us! (I do not know, I am not Arab, but only someone who opens a new book (for me) to read. And what do I read? Yes, a legend, but one made so cleverly by the author that much of it almost becomes realism. Magical realism, we might say. A writer’s technique that mixes reality with fantasy. Here it is done so well that both the factual and the extraordinary become something that can sound realistic. Put very strongly, by me.

Often one finds a partly strong social criticism that follows the story. And I find that Coelho does this masterfully in this book, as a book within the book. Santiago is a completely ordinary and down-to-earth shepherd. He sets out on a—in its starting-point’s realism—almost hopeless journey to reach a goal he does not even know how geographically far away is. But along the way he is also to, he is told, carry out his own story. (Or legend, if one wants. For he wants—like so many others of us—to find a treasure. In other words: become rich!

Mankind’s dream through all ages? (Perhaps that is why we play the lottery every week?) In any case:

Santiago meets many who want to convince him that his dreams, his journey, cannot be realized. We can begin with his father and the crystal merchant, perhaps first and foremost. Note that these are OLDER people who for one reason or another have let fear get the upper hand over their own dreams. In that sense Coelho is critical not only of those who have failed in their own lives, but even more of those who try to push their own bitterness onto those who actively want to strive to follow their dreams of reaching a goal.

Perhaps the clearest example of magical realism is in the concluding part of Part II, where Santiago is able to speak with the desert, the wind, and the sun; he persuades all these elements to help him prove to all the tribal chieftains that he is an alchemist.

In the end one can say that the magical realism is completed when a robber tells Santiago that his treasure is actually at the starting point of his journey: the shepherd’s hut in Spain. But Santiago is rewarded by the universe for his stubbornness and enthusiasm. The universe was on his side because of his own ability to learn patience, to believe in himself, to keep going and complete his journey, and because of how he masters the obstacles that arise along the way.

The book is well written. It becomes beautiful because love plays such a large role (as it does in all good books?) Here love is called Fátima. (A random name? Hardly. Muhammad’s eldest daughter was called Fátima.) Other things too have been placed into the book, done with some intention or other, I think! Without it mattering for reading the book, which, like all legends, fairy tales and the like, is pleasant reading and absolutely entertaining. But this old king. The King of Salem. Namely: Salem, which is believing Jews’ name for Jerusalem, or rather: Shalem, which means peace in Hebrew. And so on, and so on. Even the wind he calls the Levant is known in Asia and North Africa with the meaning “the land of the sunrise” (Egypt is one of these countries according to tradition), where the pyramids are.....

And these stones Urim and Thummim are, according to tradition, stones, or precious objects, which the high priest in ancient Israel used as tools for God’s WILL.

In any case: A book I will not forget. For several reasons. And none of them are religious!

I mentioned that the person who made me aware of the book—my youngest daughter—had just come home from a strenuous journey around the whole eastern Australian coast. She stayed overnight in so-called hostels. I note that the name of such hostels is often Maktub.

And: When you want something, the universe will always put itself on your side. (First said here in the book by the old King of Salem.)

This book overflows with what we can use as good quotations.

If I had not noticed that Coelho himself found this name by thinking of Hemingway, I might never have got around to reading this book.

My favorite quotation can just as well end my post: “WHAT HAPPENS ONCE WILL NEVER HAPPEN AGAIN. BUT WHAT HAPPENS TWICE WILL ALWAYS HAPPEN A THIRD TIME.”

For me this was a gem of a book, because it was so different from others I have read (and I have read many! = I am old), and it was well written, and one rarely gets so much adventure for adults. Here nothing has been spared: love (fortunately), wars (unfortunately), excitement, dangerous journeys, and everything ends well as it should.(?) And it is timeless.

Nothing is mentioned so that we have a peg to hang the course of events on in time-history. No year. No world war. etc. Good!!
