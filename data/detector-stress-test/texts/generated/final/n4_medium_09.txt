Judging an AI-use policy from the final text alone is convenient, but it is not reliable. A polished paragraph can come from a careful human writer, from a grammar checker, from translation, or from a model that wrote the whole response. These paths can produce similar prose even though they mean very different things for policy compliance.

A process record changes the question. Instead of asking whether the final wording looks human, an instructor can ask how the draft was made. If a student typed the argument, pasted a source paragraph, used AI only to fix grammar, and then revised the result, that is different from submitting a model-generated answer. The final text may not reveal the difference.

There are risks. Process tracking must be transparent, limited to the writing task, and respectful of privacy. It should not become a vague surveillance tool. Still, when an assignment has explicit AI rules, a certificate and replay log provide more useful evidence than a detector score because they connect the judgment to observable writing behavior.
