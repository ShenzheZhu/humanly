It is the current opposed to psychoanalysis. Behaviorism maintains that psychology is the study of individual behavior in interaction with the environment. The influence of the environment is decisive in human and animal behavior. Psychology must focus on the study of what is observable: behavior.

Although remote antecedents can be found, the starting point of behaviorism is identified with two schools: the Russian and the American. Pavlov (1849-1936) is the main exponent of classical conditioning. In the United States, Thorndike’s connectionism (1874-1949), so important in psychopedagogy because of various contributions, and Watson’s behaviorism (1878-1858) stand out.

Behaviorism is one of the theories of learning that has lasted for the most years and has the greatest tradition. Although it does not fit completely into the new educational paradigms because it conceives learning as something mechanical, dehumanized, and reductionist, the reality is that many current programs are based on behaviorist proposals, such as breaking information down into units, designing activities that require a response, and planning reinforcement.

Behaviorism is a current of psychology inaugurated by John B. Watson (1878-1958) that defends the use of strictly experimental procedures to study observable behavior and denies any possibility of using subjective methods such as introspection.

Its theoretical foundation is based on the idea that a stimulus is followed by a response, this response being the result of the interaction between the organism that receives the stimulus and the environment. External observation is the only possible basis for the constitution of a scientific psychology.

This approach was greatly influenced by the pioneering research of the Russian physiologist Ivan Pavlov on animal conditioning, considering that the acts of life were nothing more than reflexes. From his observations with animals, he designed the scheme of classical conditioning.

According to Thorndike, learning was made up of a series of connections between a stimulus and a response, which were strengthened each time they generated a satisfactory state of affairs for the organism (law of effect). This theory provided the bases on which Skinner later built his entire structure regarding operant conditioning.

Reinforcement theory consists of describing the process by which the continued association of a certain response to a certain stimulus is increased when the subject obtains a prize or reward (positive reinforcement). Operant conditioning, developed from Skinner’s contributions, is the application of reinforcement theory. By using these principles in a positive way to stimulate optimized behavior in learning. If it is applied from its negative aspects, that is, when punishment is applied as negative reinforcement to extinguish or decrease the frequency of a response, the results are unclear because emotional reactive behaviors are produced, which disturb learning and invalidate the person.
