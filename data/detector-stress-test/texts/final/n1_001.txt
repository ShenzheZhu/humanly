Instructors should not evaluate only the final submitted text when the purpose of an assignment is to assess learning, effort, and responsible use of tools. A polished essay can demonstrate command of language, but it cannot by itself show how the student arrived at the final version. In the age of generative AI, this distinction matters. Two submissions may look equally fluent on the page while reflecting very different processes: one may come from sustained drafting and revision, while the other may be largely generated by an external system.

Considering the writing process does not mean punishing students for using technology. Instead, it allows instructors to interpret the final text in context. If a course policy permits grammar correction or translation support, then a student who uses those tools transparently should not be treated the same as a student who asks an AI system to produce the argument wholesale. Final text alone often collapses these cases into the same category because it can only reveal the surface of the prose.

Process evidence can also make assessment fairer. Students who write slowly, revise heavily, or use permitted support can show the work behind the submission. Instructors can then focus less on guessing whether a text "sounds like AI" and more on whether the student's process matched the rules of the task. The final product still matters, but it should be read alongside evidence of drafting, revision, and tool use. For writing assignments, evaluating both outcome and process is more consistent with the educational goal: understanding not only what students submitted, but how they learned and made decisions while composing it.
