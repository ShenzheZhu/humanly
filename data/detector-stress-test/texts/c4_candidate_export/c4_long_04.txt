Summary

This paper investigates whether neural answer-generation methods can produce useful responses to non-factoid questions, using the domain of love and relationship advice as a concrete and deliberately challenging testbed. Non-factoid questions differ fundamentally from factoid questions in that they do not have a single correct answer that can be retrieved from a knowledge base; instead, good answers require empathy, contextual understanding, and coherent generation of advice. The authors propose a neural generation approach, building on encoder-decoder sequence-to-sequence architectures, and introduce mechanisms intended to improve the relevance and quality of generated answers in this setting. They describe the model, the data used to train it (a corpus of question-and-advice pairs), and the modifications made to better handle the open-ended nature of the task. Evaluation combines automatic metrics with human judgment to assess whether the generated advice is sensible, relevant, and helpful, and the paper discusses the practical and conceptual challenges of generating subjective, non-factoid content.

Strengths

The paper tackles an interesting and underexplored problem. Most work on question answering at the time focused on factoid questions with verifiable answers, so the attention to non-factoid, advice-style questions fills a genuine gap and broadens the scope of what neural QA systems are asked to do. The choice of love advice as a domain, while attention-grabbing, is a reasonable and honest example of the broader class of subjective, open-ended questions, and the authors are candid about why it is difficult.

The methodological contribution, adapting neural generation architectures to better suit non-factoid answers, is sensible and the proposed mechanisms are motivated by the specific demands of the task. The recognition that generic sequence-to-sequence models tend to produce bland, safe, and repetitive responses is well founded, and the paper's attempts to mitigate this are aligned with a real and important problem in neural text generation.

The evaluation includes human assessment, which is essential for a task where automatic metrics are known to correlate poorly with answer quality. The authors deserve credit for not relying solely on metrics like BLEU, and for engaging directly with the question of whether the outputs are actually useful to a reader.

Weaknesses

The central difficulty is evaluation, and despite the inclusion of human judgment, the paper does not fully resolve how to assess the quality of subjective advice rigorously. Advice quality is inherently contested, and the criteria, annotator guidelines, and inter-annotator agreement need to be reported in more detail for the human evaluation to be convincing. Without this, it is hard to know how much weight to place on the reported improvements.

There is also a concern about depth versus novelty of the generated content. Neural generation systems of this kind tend to produce fluent but superficial text, and it is not clear from the paper whether the model offers advice that is meaningfully responsive to the specifics of each question or whether it falls back on generic, broadly applicable platitudes that happen to read as coherent. A qualitative analysis distinguishing genuinely tailored answers from safe generalities would clarify this.

The reliance on automatic metrics for part of the evaluation is a limitation the authors acknowledge, but the disconnect between such metrics and actual usefulness in this domain is severe enough that the quantitative results should be interpreted cautiously. Relatedly, the dataset and its provenance shape the model's behavior in ways that warrant more discussion, including potential biases in the advice it learns to reproduce.

Finally, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the ethical dimension. A system that dispenses relationship advice could affect vulnerable users, and the responsibilities and risks of deploying such a system are touched on only lightly.

Questions for the Authors

First, what were the precise criteria and instructions given to human evaluators, and what was the inter-annotator agreement? How robust are the reported improvements to different evaluation protocols?

Second, can you provide qualitative examples that distinguish answers genuinely tailored to the question from generic advice that would apply to almost any query? How often does each occur?

Third, how does the training corpus shape the content and potential biases of the generated advice, and what steps, if any, were taken to address harmful or low-quality source material?

Fourth, given the weak correlation between automatic metrics and usefulness here, how much should readers rely on the quantitative results?

Fifth, what ethical safeguards would you consider necessary before such a system could be used by real users seeking advice?

Additional Comments

Beyond the points above, a few additions would help. The paper should report more detail on the dataset, including its size, source, and any filtering, since the character of the advice the model produces is largely inherited from this corpus. Providing a taxonomy of question types, and breaking down performance by type, would reveal whether the model handles some categories of relationship questions far better than others. It would also be informative to include strong but simple baselines, such as a retrieval system that returns the most relevant human-written answer, since for subjective advice retrieval can be a surprisingly competitive and safer alternative to generation. A discussion of failure modes, with representative examples of unhelpful or inappropriate outputs, would give a balanced view and is particularly important in a sensitive domain. Finally, I would encourage the authors to engage more deeply with the question of what "success" means here: fluency, relevance, emotional appropriateness, and actionable usefulness are distinct dimensions, and the paper would be stronger for measuring them separately rather than collapsing them into a single judgment. These refinements would make the evaluation more interpretable and the contribution more credible.

Overall Recommendation

This paper explores a creative and worthwhile problem, extending neural answer generation to the difficult terrain of subjective, non-factoid questions, and it takes the important step of incorporating human evaluation. The work is honest about its challenges and addresses a real limitation of generic generation models. However, the evaluation methodology is not yet rigorous enough to firmly establish the quality of the generated advice, the depth and specificity of the outputs are not clearly demonstrated, and the ethical considerations are underdeveloped. I view this as a borderline paper. Its value lies in opening up an interesting direction and in its candid treatment of a hard problem, but the current evidence for success is incomplete. I would lean toward weak acceptance if the authors substantially strengthen the human evaluation and provide a clearer qualitative analysis of answer quality, and otherwise see it as a promising but not-yet-conclusive contribution.
